## The question is — Why And — why did he want to show it

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*wHsjr0I2wu3qcAIzc7X6WA.jpeg)

General Mark Milley and the Joint Chiefs of Staff, photo courtesy of Wikimedia Commons

Like most Americans, and no doubt like the vast majority of American veterans, I found the recently released “Bedminster tape” quite disturbing. It seems to reveal Donald Trump casually, even laughingly, sitting with some advisors and guests next to the pool at his New Jersey country club and discussing a classified war plan apparently related to Iran. Trump shouldn’t have had such a plan and should not have been discussing it with others. His motive was seemingly to argue that he was preventing the Joint Chiefs of Staff from a little military adventurism.

Given that the released tape is only two minutes long, and what is missing is the beginning and end of the discussion, its full context remains speculative. In all likelihood Special Counsel Jack Smith knows that context in all its dimensions. But let me offer some speculation of my own.

As has been widely reported, during the days leading up to and following the attack on the U.S. Capitol on January 6, 2021, General Mark Milley, the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, was seriously concerned about what other steps Trump might take in his increasingly desperate efforts to stay in office. Among Milley’s concerns were that Trump might order an attack on another country — in essence the old *Wag the Dog* scenario from the 1997 movie. Milley evidently even had concerns about a nuclear attack.

But given Trump’s intense dislike of Iran, as well as his dubious but obvious connections with their main Gulf rival, Saudi Arabia, Milley was likely more concerned about some dangerous escalation in that troubled region. Trump had not responded to the September 2019 Iranian attack on the Saudi oil facility at Abqaiq, but had then ordered the killing of Iranian General Qasem Soleimani, commander of the Iranian Quds Force, in January 2020.

It is unclear exactly what specific plans regarding Iran Trump was holding during the taped call, but he clearly had something he had received from General Milley. As the transcript of the call shows, grabbing the document Trump says: “Well, with Milley — uh, let me see that, I’ll show you an example. He said that I wanted to attack Iran. Isn’t that amazing I have a big pile of papers, this thing just came up. Look. This was him.jpg).”

The document Trump was waving was clearly classified as he then comments: “This was done by the military, given to me. I think we can, probably — see as president, I could have declassified. Now I can’t, you know, but this is still a secret.”

==So, the central question is, why did Trump have what appears to be a conceptual — and no doubt highly classified — war plan for Iran Did he have it because General Milley simply sent it to Trump “FYI,” or did he have it because he had requested it The former possibility is unlikely; the latter quite disturbing==.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*fP4r7LIJn4fVsDsqRlasUg.jpeg)

U.S. paratroopers training in Germany, photo courtesy of Wikimedia Commons

Throughout its years of existence, the Joint Chiefs of Staff have routinely prepared war plans to at the very least have a starting point for any possible contingency. It’s a practice that goes back decades before there even was a JCS in its current configuration. And sometimes those old plans even addressed highly unlikely contingencies.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*CIfODmlsCVWzjpS3gAqndg.jpeg)

The Joint Chiefs of Staff in WWII, photo courtesy of Wikimedia Commons

In the 1930’s, for example, the Joint Planning Committee, the predecessor to the current JCS, prepared a very comprehensive set of plans known as the “Color Coded War Plans.” Why “color coded” Because each was assigned a color depending on the country the conflict was assumed to involve. War Plan Orange, for instance, envisioned a war in the Pacific against a rising Japanese Empire. There was also, believe it or not, a War Plan Red that covered a possible war with the British Empire! That was no doubt quite far-fetched, but someone on the planning staff must have remembered that we had experienced trouble with the British in the past, including that little dust-up in 1776 and the following one in 1812. You never know.

The combination of the war plans for Japan and Britain resulted in the development of War Plan Red-Orange, an exercise that showed that a war in two distant and distinct theaters would likely be beyond the American capability. Still, planning continued and the “Color-coded” plans were eventually replaced by the “Rainbow Plans,” with “Rainbow Five” essentially being the basis for the two-front, global war fought after Pearl Harbor.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*_m7MHkUD7SeForcILPVprQ.jpeg)

Marines landing on Iwo Jima in February 1945, photo courtesy of Wikimedia Commons

Institutional habits being what they are, be assured that today’s Pentagon has a whole set of war plans. They would be quite derelict in their duties were that not the case.

During the 2020 campaign Trump frequently asserted that he was the first president since Dwight Eisenhower to not involve the country in a war. He also claimed to have opposed the 2003 invasion of Iraq, although several of his statements from that period contradicted the claim. But clearly Trump was trying to present himself as a commander-in-chief not prone to using military force, a theme that meshed with his isolationist and “America first” positions.

Trump was, therefore, most unhappy with Milley’s suggestions that in Trump’s desperation to stay in office he might start an unnecessary and unwinnable conflict. So, back to the question: Why did Trump have a classified war plan for Iran

Would Milley have sent him such a plan unsolicited Possibly, but highly unlikely. War plans are detailed and complicated, chocked full of annexes and appendices regarding deployment schedules and logistics. According to numerous sources, Trump did not like to read more than a page — if that. So just sending him such a plan would have been unlikely. ==And Trump would have likely been unable to understand it in any event.==

It is possible that Milley and his planners from the JCS J-3 directorate might have brought the plan over to brief him and then, at some point, left it with him. But leaving war plans lying around is not common practice, although as is now well-known Trump was not a common president.

But what if Trump had asked for it at some point during the period after the election — particularly following January 6th This would certainly have created a serious concern for Milley, especially given his phone call with House Speaker Nancy Pelosi after the Capitol attack where she stated, “This is bad, but who knows what he might do He’s crazy. You know he’s crazy. He’s been crazy for a long time.”

Milley reportedly replied to the Speaker, “I agree with you on everything.” Accordingly, a request for a war plan from Trump would have set off numerous alarm bells, especially coming from a president who was both uninterested and uneducated in national security issues.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*hA_07EQBVtIqmVwpa4bSyg.jpeg)

January 6th insurrection, photo courtesy of Wikimedia Commons

It is, therefore, likely that in Trump’s “light-hearted” discussion with his staff and guests at Bedminster, he pulled out the war plan, which would have almost certainly carried a “Top Secret” security classification, to suggest that it was *actually Milley* who wanted to use the moment to start a conflict. To repeat, Trump first defends himself against Milley’s concern that Trump might launch some provocative attack saying Milley gave him a plan for some attack, concluding, “this was him.”

Trump then adds the part of the discussion of greatest interest to Special Counsel Jack Smith, “This was done by the military, given to me. I think we can, probably — see as president, I could have declassified. Now I can’t, you know, but this is still a secret.” If what he held in his hand was instead — as Trump subsequently has argued — newspaper articles or building plans, well…those are always unclassified. They are also not provided to the president by the military.

Among the other things disturbing in the Bedminster tape is the apparent lack of awareness by Trump of what the military actually does as a matter of routine. The tape suggests that Trump believed the Pentagon, and Milley, had recently drafted the plan and rushed it to him hoping he would give some sort of “go” signal. As some have commented, when it came to being president, Trump sat organizationally atop the executive branch but managerially detached from it. As others have noted, he was indifferent to — even contemptuous of — security classifications and clearances. It is this combination of detachment, indifference, disinterest, and — we should add — ==incompetence that the nation must avoid in the future,== and that Donald Trump must be held accountable for in the present.
