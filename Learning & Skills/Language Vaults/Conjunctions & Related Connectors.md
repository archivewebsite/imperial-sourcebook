## 🔹 True Conjunctions
| Conjunction      | Type          | Definition                                                                 | Example                                                                 |
|------------------|---------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| And              | Coordinating  | Joins words, phrases, or clauses of equal importance; adds information.    | I bought apples, and she bought oranges.                                |
| But              | Coordinating  | Joins contrasting ideas of equal importance.                               | He studied hard, but he didn't pass the exam.                           |
| Or               | Coordinating  | Presents alternatives or choices.                                          | You can stay here, or you can come with us.                             |
| Both ... and     | Correlative   | Connects two equal elements, emphasizing that both are true or included.   | She is both a talented musician and a dedicated teacher.                |
| Either ... or    | Correlative   | Presents a choice between two alternatives.                                | Either you apologize, or I will leave.                                  |
| Neither ... nor  | Correlative   | Negates two alternatives simultaneously.                                   | Neither the manager nor the employees were informed.                    |
| Although / Though| Subordinating | Introduces a contrast or unexpected result; means "in spite of the fact that". | Although it was raining, we went for a walk.                          |
| Unless           | Subordinating | Introduces a condition; means "except if".                                 | You will miss the train unless you leave now.                           |
| Whereas          | Subordinating | Highlights a contrast between two facts or ideas.                          | He prefers quiet evenings, whereas she loves going out.                 |
| Because          | Subordinating | Introduces a reason or cause.                                              | She stayed home because she was feeling ill.                            |
| If               | Subordinating | Introduces a condition.                                                    | If you finish your work, you can leave early.                           |

## 🔹 Conjunctive Adverbs & Transitional Phrases
*(Note: These are NOT conjunctions. They connect ideas across sentences or independent clauses and require different punctuation.)*

| Word/Phrase      | Category             | Definition                                                                 | Example                                                                 |
|------------------|----------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Furthermore      | Conjunctive Adverb   | Adds information; means "in addition" or "moreover".                       | The plan is too expensive; furthermore, it is impractical.              |
| Likewise         | Conjunctive Adverb   | Indicates similarity; means "in the same way" or "similarly".              | The first experiment failed. Likewise, the second attempt yielded no results. |
| However          | Conjunctive Adverb   | Introduces a contrast or exception.                                        | I wanted to go; however, I was too tired.                               |
| Therefore        | Conjunctive Adverb   | Shows a result or conclusion.                                              | He didn't study; therefore, he failed the test.                         |
| For example      | Transitional Phrase  | Introduces a specific illustration of a general statement.                 | Many fruits are rich in vitamin C; for example, oranges and strawberries. |
| In brief / In short | Transitional Phrase | Signals a summary or conclusion.                                           | The project was over budget and behind schedule. In brief, it failed.   |
| Besides          | Preposition / Adverb | Means "in addition to" or "apart from". (Not a conjunction.)               | Besides English, she speaks French and Japanese.                        |
| Eventually       | Adverb of Time       | Means "in the end" or "after a long time". (Not a conjunction.)            | After hours of negotiation, they eventually reached an agreement.       |
| Anyway           | Discourse Marker     | Used to return to a topic, dismiss something, or continue despite obstacles. | I know it's risky, but I'm going to try anyway.                       |

### 📝 Punctuation Notes
- **Coordinating conjunctions** (`and, but, or, so, yet, for, nor`): Use a comma before them when joining two independent clauses.  
  `I wanted to go, but I was tired.`
- **Subordinating conjunctions** (`although, unless, because, if, whereas`): No comma needed if the clause comes second. Use a comma if it comes first.  
  `Although it rained, we went out.` / `We went out although it rained.`
- **Correlative conjunctions** (`both...and, either...or, neither...nor`): Ensure parallel structure.  
  `She is both a writer and an editor.` (Not: `both writes and an editor`)
- **Conjunctive adverbs** (`furthermore, likewise, however, therefore`): Use a semicolon before and a comma after when linking independent clauses.  
  `He was late; however, he still got the job.`
