## From attacks on two fronts — corporate and government

![National Guard soldiers at the US Capitol, photo from VOA News.](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*pp78wFPEWrSk2EO_1_JB2Q.avif)

National Guard soldiers at the US Capitol, photo from VOA News at https://www.voanews.com/a/usa\_us-capitol-riot-hearing-3-hour-delay-deploying-guard-troops/6202824.html

Anyone who delves deeply into political philosophy recognizes it is a difficult, often turgid, topic. The challenge it presents is magnified as most of the original philosophical thoughts were penned centuries ago and often written in language no longer in common use.

Accordingly, as an undergraduate student, I found reading the assigned works on the topic quite the challenge. Fortunately, however, I had a teacher who was a former Rhodes Scholar and possessed a great talent for translating the old obtuse writings into understandable, contemporary English. His lectures were much more informative than my often-tortured efforts to comprehend the original texts.

The great political thinkers extending back to the 17th century were shaped by many, sometimes conflicting thoughts about the conditions they observed including civil war, increasingly powerful monarchies and privileged classes, social inequalities, and what were the rights of the individual. The one element of these studies and their resultant conclusions that caught my attention in these old scripts was the concept of the “Social Contract,” the fundamental understanding between a people and their government regarding the perceptions and rules governing their relationship. There were several of the more famous philosophers whose views seemed to resonate over time, often shaping great events.

![Thomas Hobbes, photot courtesy of Wikimedia Commons,](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*tkZhUTtfiHCS1qwYXxEPFQ.jpeg)

Thomas Hobbes, photot courtesy of Wikimedia Commons, https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Thomas\_Hobbes\_(portrait).jpg

The British political philosopher Thomas Hobbes (1588–1679) believed that mankind tended to be in a constant state of war, that human nature was fundamentally selfish and flawed, and that such a condition necessitated a strong, even absolute, government to maintain order and prevent chaos.

![John Locke, photo courtesy of Wikimedia Commons](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*iZjM2azhC-HfGVms_dWMSA.jpeg)

John Locke, photo courtesy of Wikimedia Commons, https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:JohnLocke.png

By contrast, John Locke (1632–1704) believed that man had certain natural rights — such as life, liberty, and property — and that governments existed to protect these natural rights and were regulated by the consent of the governed. And should the government fail to properly do so, the people retained the right to change or abolish it.

![Baron de Montesquieu, portrait courtesy of Wikimedia Commons](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1280/format:webp/1*hXAsq6THg-XRXaRXGx682g.png)

Baron de Montesquieu, portrait courtesy of Wikimedia Commons, https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Montesquieu\_1.png

There was obviously the need for balance in this relationship. Baron de Montesquieu (1689–1755) argued that protection against a tyrannical government required a governing structure with a separation of powers, one having distinct legislative, executive, and judicial branches, each checking and balancing the others, a structure addressing Montesquieu’s concerns about the tendency for governmental over-reach.

![Jean-Jacques Rousseau, portrait courtesy of Wikimedia Commons](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*wjAPfQ0dnvhIVlW_NkMClw.jpeg)

Jean-Jacques Rousseau, portrait courtesy of Wikimedia Commons, https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Jean-Jacques\_Rousseau\_(painted\_portrait).jpg

Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) emphasized direct democracy and the concept of the “general will.” Accordingly, he was “in general” mistrustful of a *representative* democracy, feeling it could never adequately reflect and protect the true “general will” of those selecting the representatives.

![ohn Stewart Mill, portrait courtesy of Wikimedia Commons](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1280/format:webp/1*Ub7e511iTMjZ8VJBxLg1UA.jpeg)

John Stewart Mill, portrait courtesy of Wikimedia Commons, https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Stuart\_Mill\_G\_F\_Watts.jpg

Later, there was John Stewart Mill (1806–1873) who strongly advocated individual liberty while acknowledging that it was not absolute and government was needed to ensure individuals, in pursuit of their own self-interest, would not harm others. This acknowledged the need for social rules and institutions to protect individuals while promoting social order.

![The Constitutional Convention, painting courtesy of Wikimedia Commons](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*pzaSFAo7IARtZOnNAhwPig.jpeg)

The Constitutional Convention, painting courtesy of Wikimedia Commons, https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Flickr\_-\_USCapitol\_-\_Signing\_of\_the\_Constitution.jpg

In the late 1700's, the thoughts of these great thinkers found a real world application across the Atlantic. When the American Founding Fathers gathered to draft a constitution, they leaned heavily on the thoughts of their near-contemporaries, Locke and Montesquieu, by crafting a government that acknowledged Locke’s “natural rights” with its own eventual (if not immediate) “Bill of Rights,” echoing the famous words penned by Thomas Jefferson in the Declaration of Independence that all men are “endowed by their Creator with certain inalienable rights.”

![Thomas Jefferson, portrait courtesy of Wikimedi Commons](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1280/format:webp/1*nB3L9xZbLkZoHHJE6ZUA8g.jpeg)

Thomas Jefferson, portrait courtesy of Wikimedi Commons, https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Official\_Presidential\_portrait\_of\_Thomas\_Jefferson\_(by\_Rembrandt\_Peale,\_1800).jpg

To protect those rights and break from the monarchical rule they had fought against, they created a representative democracy of separated powers between three co-equal branches of government, each serving as a check on the other two. Further, they added one more layer of protection against any tyrannical tendency by creating a federal system leaving state and local governments to deal with issues closest to the interests of the governed.

It was an ingenious solution, but one that was somewhat unevenly and inconsistently applied as witnessed seventy years later in a terrible civil war. That conflict was largely the result of what historian Shelby Foote noted was our inability of the moment, “to do the thing we really have a genius for, which is compromise.” Compromise was at the heart of the American constitutional system. And it should remain there.

Mill argued that social rules evolve over time, and therefore the relationship between a government and the governed needed to be flexible. In other words, the dimensions of the social contract should change in a manner that might on occasion enhance the “general welfare,” or at other times possibly necessitate greater government control to ensure social order. The U.S. governmental structure allowed for this, most prominently in its provision to amend the Constitution, something that has happened twenty-seven times.

But perhaps the most significant expansion of the social contract occurred in the wake of the Great Depression that afflicted the country in the 1930’s. In response to a severe economic disruption, the social contract indeed expanded beyond mere protection. President Franklin Roosevelt saw that existing conditions required the government to not only safeguard against external threats and political oppression, but also to protect individual rights and promote a level of economic security for its citizens. This was best reflected in the Social Security Act of 1938, and later by President Lyndon Johnson with the establishment of Medicare in 1965.

The American social contract, as Mill foresaw, has indeed evolved over time. As those changes have impacted the “general will” recognized by Rousseau, they have resulted in governmental changes regarding “life and liberty” as argued by Locke. Yet at this moment in the American social contract, what we see is a growing challenge to Locke’s expectation of limited government and Mill’s expectation of institutions promoting social order.

These changes to the American social contract are rooted in two locations: a government that is expanding its scope of action into areas that limit individual liberty, challenge the concept of federalism, and significantly expand control by the executive branch; and an economic structure driven by a corporate culture that increasingly values “shareholder value” and profit over any attention to social responsibility. Both are dangerous trends challenging the existing “social contract” and threatening the existing “social order.”

Let us start with the changed corporate culture. Historically, corporate leaders believed their responsibilities extended, as one once commented, to achieving four things. First, to produce a good product that satisfied their customers. Second, to provide their employees a safe work environment. Third, to ensure their employees were adequately compensated in the present and had the prospect of a comfortable life after their working years. And fourth, to provide the corporation’s shareholders a reasonable return on their investment. In this view, addressing the first three would result in the fourth. Of note, two of these four rank-ordered objectives directly related to the employees.

But things have changed rather radically since 1980. The current corporate attitude has turned those old priorities on their head. Since the advent and spread in the 1980’s of the “shareholder value” philosophy of former General Electric CEO Jack Welch, employees have essentially been moved from the “asset” to the “liability” side of the corporate balance sheet, and achieving shareholder returns has become by far the primary business motivation.

This change has manifested itself in numerous ways — two of them quite significant. First, the defined benefit (DB) pension plan has basically been eliminated and replaced with defined contribution (DC) retirement plans. In 1980, nearly 40% of American workers were covered by a DB (some measurements suggest a much higher number). Today that number is under 10%. The DB percentage is much higher among public sector workers where about 75% are still covered with such a plan.

The most common DC program is a 401(K). But as the “DC” moniker shows, unlike a DB funded retirement both the employer and the employee contribute to this program. And as was seen in the “great recession” of 2008, a 401(K), which is usually invested and managed by a financial entity, can quickly lose value during periods of economic distress. As one employee tearfully said to a media interviewer during that disruptive period of 2008, her 401(K) had lost half its value, becoming a “201(K).”

As corporations and financial institutions argue, there are indeed certain advantages to a DC plan. In general, the money vests earlier, and it is portable as employees can take it with them when they change jobs in whole or part. However, it is less certain than a DB and is dependent on economic factors beyond the influence of either the host company or its employees. That means employers have a lesser interest in the well-being of their employees, who will quite naturally have a lesser interest in the success of their employer. Over time, this diminished mutual interest between employees and employers hurts the business prospects of the latter and the sense of security for the former.

This economic dimension is distinct, of course, from the thoughts of the legendary political philosophers regarding their conception of the “social contract. ” But at the economic level, where the pursuit of “life and liberty” finds practical application in a capitalist society, it has created a major change over the years creating considerable personal insecurity.

The second part of the changed social contract agreement between employees, employers, and the government involves health care. A benefit that once came with employment has also evolved to an employee contribution plan with the size of the employee contribution related to the comprehensiveness of the selected health program. Once again, those in the public sector, especially the military, have a much more generous health program provided at little or no cost. But for the majority, health care has become a major concern regarding both cost and access. This has made it a contentious issue over the past three decades, and another source of individual insecurity.

What of the traditional American “Social Contract” are we seeing now

The most worrisome alteration of the American social contract is occuring within the government itself in a manner that would have greatly disturbed Locke, Montesquieu, and Mill. We have reached a point following the 2024 election where the traditional “social contract” has seriously come into question, mainly because the traditional governmental “checks and balances” are under almost daily challenge in numerous domains where it has never been in question. There is a growing sense that the U.S. federal government is greatly altering the American “social contract” by steadily expanding its reach and authority, enabled by a legislature that is far too meek and a judiciary that is insufficiently attentive. The result is individuals are relinquishing many of the freedoms and protections they have long enjoyed despite recent polling data suggesting this is not a relinquishment that has the consent of the majority of the governed.

![President Theodore Roosevelt, portrait courtesy of Wikimedia Commons](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1196/format:webp/1*gPmHNBWXY9PAFxb5fUzG2w.jpeg)

President Theodore Roosevelt, portrait courtesy of Wikimedia Commons, https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Theodore\_Roosevelt\_by\_Jacques\_Reich.jpg

Perhaps the greatest concern here — although certainly not the sole concern — is the steady attack on established institutions, in recent times derogatorily called the “deep state.” Since the days of Civil Service Commissioner Theodore Roosevelt early in the previous century, there has been a broad effort to develop an American Civil Service that is professional and apolitical, that follows the instructions of the political party in power while simultaneously advising it on legal and procedural boundaries as well as long-accepted and respected precedents.

In many instances, particularly in major offices and institutions created to be apolitical, statutorily tenured leaders are being replaced for clearly political reasons and replaced with those having an equally clear political agenda. This has been widely seen in the departments of State, Justice, and Defense where appointed and apolitical leaders in positions having statutorily established tenures as long as ten-years have been summarily dismissed. This has been particularly worrisome in institutions such as the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) and the military services of the Defense Department (DoD).

![Army National Guard deployed to Los Angeles in June 2025, photo courtesy of Wikimedia Commons](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*pgudrgojxsk17uUi4-kIOA.jpeg)

Army National Guard deployed to Los Angeles in June 2025, photo courtesy of Wikimedia Commons, https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Los\_Angeles\_riots\_-\_June\_2025\_-\_20250613.jpg

In addition, we are now seeing an attack on the basic concept of federalism. The current administration has sent federal agents and even troops under specious reasons into locations where they were not requested by state and local authorities. This has been most prominently seen in California and the District of Columbia, but other localities such as Chicago and Baltimore have been threatened with similar, unwanted interventions.

Montesquieu’s concept of the separation of governmental powers, with distinct legislative, executive, and judicial branches, each checking and balancing the others, found a comfortable reflection in the governmental structure established by the U.S. Constitution. But today we see a legislative branch that watches supinely as much of its traditional, balancing function goes unexercised, unenforced, or simply ignored.

The judiciary has attempted a more diligent effort, but has nonetheless displayed a disturbing tendency to allow actions it has found unlawful to continue while executive branch appeals continue at sloth-like speed. More bothersome, at the Supreme Court level a supposedly conservative court has shown a liberal-like tendency by discovering executive latitudes that were previously unknown. The most disturbing of these being the uncovering of a previously unknown executive immunity, one that would have been quite useful to the chief executive himself during the Nixon administration.

In full combination, this all suggests that the “social contract” we once applauded in the United States, offering ourselves as a realistic representation of the concept, and encouraging others to emulate it (with some considerable success), seems to be receding at a moment where it is seriously needed.

As originally conceived, the social contract was a relatively straight-forward yet implicit agreement between the governing and the governed. The latter agreed to limit some of its liberties and inalienable rights in exchange for the safety, security, and social order provided by the former. But this agreement came with safeguards protecting the governed against tendencies toward over-reach that history suggested were often attempted by those governing. And in recent years, this contract acquired an additional economic dimension when modern history indicated that the famous Scot economist Adam Smith’s concept of a benevolent “invisible hand” could occasionally suffer from arthritic dysfunction.

The enduring quest for a “social contract” lay in seeking balance between freedom and disorder, between overly permissive liberty or overly oppressive tyranny. We now find ourselves at a moment where we must reset the social fulcrum, because current conditions indicate it has been relocated in a most unhealthy direction.PoliticsPhilosophyGovernmentLawSociety
