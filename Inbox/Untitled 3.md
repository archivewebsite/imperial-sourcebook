The seven deadly sins today do not usually look like hooded medieval villains whispering in Latin. They look like normal life with better branding: productivity apps, luxury ads, dating profiles, comment sections, binge-watching queues, “personal brands,” and corporations pretending extraction is innovation. Civilization, naturally, put a touchscreen on vice.

The traditional list is **pride, greed, lust, envy, gluttony, wrath, and sloth**. In Christian theology they are “capital” or “deadly” not because each act instantly ruins you, but because they are root dispositions that generate other bad actions. Britannica traces the classic seven to Gregory the Great in the 6th century and later elaboration by Thomas Aquinas. ([Encyclopedia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/seven-deadly-sins "Seven deadly sins | In Order, List, Definition, History, Names, & Examples | Britannica"))

## 1. Pride today: the self as a sacred brand

Old pride meant excessive self-love: thinking more of yourself than is true. Today it often looks like **identity absolutism**: “I am not merely someone with opinions, I am my opinions, and disagreement is violence.”

Modern pride appears as personal branding, résumé spirituality, moral superiority, status politics, expert cosplay, and the refusal to be corrected. It is the influencer saying “living my truth” when they mean “please validate my monetized personality.” It is also the executive who mistakes confidence for wisdom, the activist who cannot admit complexity, the intellectual who uses knowledge as a weapon, and the ordinary person who cannot say, “I was wrong.”

Pride is the master sin because it blocks repair. Greed can be restrained, wrath can cool, envy can be confessed. Pride refuses the diagnosis. It says, “There is no problem. I am the standard.”

Its modern antidote is not self-hatred. It is **humility**, meaning accurate self-knowledge. You are neither trash nor a demigod with Wi-Fi. You are limited, dependent, gifted, ridiculous, and responsible.

## 2. Greed today: extraction with a mission statement

Old greed meant immoderate desire for wealth or possessions. Today it is broader: **the belief that everything should be convertible into gain**.

Modern greed looks like corporations squeezing workers while calling it efficiency, billionaires treating taxation as a personal insult, landlords turning shelter into a speculative game, platforms monetizing attention, and people optimizing every hobby into a side hustle because apparently joy must now file quarterly reports.

It is not only rich people hoarding money. That is merely the premium edition. Greed also appears in ordinary life as the inability to enjoy “enough.” A person can be greedy for attention, praise, credentials, influence, followers, beauty, control, or even victimhood.

The OECD’s _Society at a Glance 2024_ treats income and wealth inequality as central social indicators, which is a polite institutional way of saying that distribution matters because societies fray when people experience the economy as rigged. ([OECD](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/society-at-a-glance-2024_918d8db3-en/full-report/income-and-wealth-inequalities_7ac4178f.html "Income and wealth inequalities: Society at a Glance 2024 | OECD"))

The antidote is **charity** or generosity. Not performative generosity. Not “I donated publicly and now my soul has a receipt.” Real generosity means refusing to make your appetite the organizing principle of the world.

## 3. Lust today: people reduced to consumable experiences

Old lust meant disordered sexual desire. Today it is better understood as **using desire to turn people into objects**.

This does not mean sex is bad. That would be a lazy reading, and humanity has already produced enough lazy readings to power a small nation. Lust is not desire itself. Lust is desire detached from personhood, responsibility, tenderness, and reality.

Modern lust looks like swipe-culture disposability, porn-shaped expectations, emotional manipulation, parasocial obsession, “situationships” where nobody has the courage to say what they want, and beauty treated as a market obligation. It also appears outside sex: lust for novelty, intensity, conquest, domination, and being wanted.

The real damage is fragmentation. Lust trains the mind to ask, “What can I get from this person?” instead of “Who is this person?” That shift sounds small. It is not.

The antidote is **chastity**, but not in the cartoon sense of repression. Chastity means integrated desire: sexuality governed by respect, consent, patience, and the recognition that other people are not accessories in your private movie.

## 4. Envy today: the comparison machine

Old envy meant sadness or resentment at another person’s good fortune. Today envy has been industrialized. We built platforms where people post curated fragments of their lives, then everyone else compares those fragments to their own unedited interior mess. A magnificent technological achievement, if the goal was mass low-grade misery.

Modern envy looks like scrolling through vacations, bodies, careers, relationships, homes, weddings, book deals, promotions, and “morning routines” filmed by people who somehow wake up camera-ready. It says: “Their success diminishes me.” It turns admiration into resentment.

Pew Research Center describes social media as central to teen life and tracks teen experiences on TikTok, Instagram, and Snapchat, including how these platforms shape daily experience for teens and parents. ([Pew Research Center](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/04/15/teens-experiences-on-tiktok-instagram-and-snapchat/ "Teens’ Experiences on TikTok, Instagram and Snapchat | Pew Research Center")) That matters because envy thrives in environments built around visibility, metrics, comparison, and public approval.

Envy is spiritually corrosive because it cannot enjoy anything directly. It sees the world through ranking. Someone else’s joy becomes evidence against you.

The antidote is **gratitude**. Not the fake kind where you write “grateful” under a beach photo. Real gratitude means learning to receive your actual life without constantly measuring it against someone else’s highlight reel.

## 5. Gluttony today: endless consumption, not merely food

Old gluttony meant excess in eating and drinking. Today it is **consumption without wisdom**.

Important distinction, since humans enjoy turning moral categories into blunt instruments: gluttony is not body size. Obesity is a public-health issue shaped by food systems, inequality, biology, stress, medicine, marketing, and environment. WHO notes that adult obesity has more than doubled since 1990 and describes “obesogenic environments” as part of a global public-health crisis. ([World Health Organization](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/obesity-and-overweight "Obesity and overweight")) Reducing that to “personal sin” would be intellectually cheap, which means someone somewhere is definitely doing it.

Modern gluttony includes binge eating, binge drinking, binge shopping, binge scrolling, binge streaming, binge outrage, binge news, binge productivity content, and binge self-improvement. It is the inability to stop taking in stimulation long enough to ask, “Is this nourishing me, or just numbing me?”

Gluttony is consumption as anesthesia. It says, “I do not want to feel this emptiness, so give me another episode, another drink, another cart checkout, another feed refresh.”

The antidote is **temperance**. Temperance is not joyless restraint. It is the art of having pleasure without being owned by it.

## 6. Wrath today: outrage as identity

Old wrath meant rage, hatred, resentment, and desire for vengeance. Today wrath has a business model.

Modern wrath looks like comment-section bloodsport, ideological purity tests, road rage, cancellation fantasies, revenge posting, political dehumanization, and the weird pleasure people take in seeing enemies humiliated. It is not anger itself. Anger can be sane. Anger can notice injustice. Wrath is anger that stops wanting repair and starts wanting destruction.

Social platforms did not invent wrath, because humans were perfectly capable of being awful with parchment and town squares. But modern media scales it, accelerates it, and rewards it. Pew notes that social media platforms sit at the center of political debates around free speech, harassment, and democracy. ([Pew Research Center](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2024/06/12/how-americans-navigate-politics-on-tiktok-x-facebook-and-instagram/?utm_source=chatgpt.com "How Americans Navigate Politics on TikTok, X ... - Pew Research Center")) The U.S. Surgeon General’s advisory on loneliness also warns about a society retreating into corners, “angry, sick, and alone,” which is bleak but regrettably not subtle. ([HHS.gov](https://www.hhs.gov/sites/default/files/surgeon-general-social-connection-advisory.pdf "Our Epidemic of Loneliness and Isolation"))

Wrath feels righteous because it gives the self a clean story: I am good, they are evil, therefore cruelty is justice. That is why it is so addictive.

The antidote is **patience**, plus disciplined justice. Patience does not mean passivity. It means refusing to let rage become your operating system.

## 7. Sloth today: despair disguised as comfort

Old sloth is often misunderstood as laziness. The older idea, especially _acedia_, is deeper: a refusal of meaningful effort, a deadening of the will, a sadness toward the good. In less antique language: “I know what matters, and I cannot bring myself to care.”

Modern sloth looks like doomscrolling, chronic avoidance, passive cynicism, learned helplessness, emotional absenteeism, spiritual numbness, and the habit of outsourcing every judgment to the crowd. It also looks like saying “everything is broken” because that conveniently excuses you from doing anything small and useful.

Here is the twist: modern sloth often coexists with overwork. A person can be professionally frantic and spiritually inert. WHO and ILO estimated that long working hours led to 745,000 deaths from stroke and ischemic heart disease in 2016, and found higher health risks for people working at least 55 hours per week. ([World Health Organization](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2021-long-working-hours-increasing-deaths-from-heart-disease-and-stroke-who-ilo "Long working hours increasing deaths from heart disease and stroke: WHO, ILO")) So no, the cure for sloth is not “work more until your organs file a complaint.” Sloth is not the absence of busyness. It is the absence of rightly ordered care.

The antidote is **diligence**: steady, meaningful effort toward what is actually good. Not hustle. Diligence. The distinction matters, despite the economy’s best efforts to murder it.

## What makes them “deadly” now?

They are deadly because each one shrinks the soul.

Pride shrinks reality to the size of the ego.  
Greed shrinks value to possession.  
Lust shrinks people to use.  
Envy shrinks joy to comparison.  
Gluttony shrinks desire to consumption.  
Wrath shrinks justice to revenge.  
Sloth shrinks life to avoidance.

The common pattern is disordered love. Each sin takes something basically good and makes it ultimate. Self-respect becomes pride. Provision becomes greed. Desire becomes lust. Admiration becomes envy. Pleasure becomes gluttony. Anger at injustice becomes wrath. Rest becomes sloth.

That is why the old framework still works. It is not mainly a list of naughty behaviors. It is a map of how human desire malfunctions.

## The modern version in one sentence each

**Pride:** “I am the main character, and reality should adapt.”  
**Greed:** “Enough is for people with no ambition.”  
**Lust:** “People are experiences I consume.”  
**Envy:** “Someone else’s happiness is an insult.”  
**Gluttony:** “More input will fix the emptiness.”  
**Wrath:** “My hatred is righteous, so my cruelty is clean.”  
**Sloth:** “Nothing matters, so do not ask anything of me.”

## The quiet moral test

The question is not “Have I committed one of these?” That is adorable. Everyone has. The better question is:

“What am I training myself to love?”

Because that is the whole game. You become what you repeatedly reward. The seven deadly sins today are not mostly dramatic collapses. They are tiny daily reinforcements: one more comparison, one more indulgence, one more contemptuous thought, one more excuse, one more little throne built for the self.

Medieval people painted demons. Modern people designed notifications. Same appetite, cleaner interface.
