## She Threw the Owl Pellets Away Herself

## What We Bury in Young Girls and Call it Womanhood

![An elderly woman watches her young grand-niece clear a dinner table in an overheated family dining room, sensing that something harsher than obedience has replaced the girl she used to be. A tense family dinner scene with an old woman studying a young woman in a synthetic blouse, her thumb bleeding under the tablecloth as practicality, ambition, and buried girlhood hang in the room. An older woman sits in uneasy silence after a family meal, watching her sharp, controlled grand-niece carry plates](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:4800/format:webp/0*5nX5pvUzjWNhhNFw)

I am watching my grand-niece Silvia scrape the baked starch off the side of a scalloped potato dish with the edge of a serving spoon that has already bent slightly at the neck, and the sound it makes against the glazed ceramic is the sort of small, dental sound that can turn a dining room hostile if everyone is already pretending not to hate one another. The room is too warm because my son-in-law insists the radiator has moods and must never be turned down abruptly, and Silvia is wearing one of those synthetic blouses that look expensive from across the room and feel like being wrapped in a supermarket bag once the body starts sweating beneath them. People say girls die at nineteen as if the thing were poetic, as if there were a candle somewhere and not just a thousand domestic adjustments performed under fluorescent light, and for years I believed it in exactly that soft, dishonest way older women sometimes believe in suffering because it flatters what we survived.

==Silvia used to keep owl pellets in a rusted biscuit tin under her bed, which her mother discovered one August while hunting for a missing gold earring and then described for weeks afterward as if the child had been storing severed fingers.== There were little vertebrae lined up on notebook paper, gray fur worked into damp clumps, a cracked bird skull no larger than the top joint of my thumb, and Silvia handled all of it with the absorbed concentration of a child who has not yet learned that adults forgive mess more easily than they forgive appetite. Her mother, who has the kind of politeness that always feels pre-chewed, could not stop mentioning it to guests in a tone that mixed embarrassment with opportunity, because some women know how to make a daughter’s oddness sound like a temporary stain they have nearly removed.

==Tonight that girl is gone, or so I think at first, because the Silvia sitting across from me speaks in the flattened office dialect of somebody already being trained by PowerPoint, and she says “supply chain visibility” and “market exposure” while carefully sliding her thumb under the cloth napkin on her lap so the others will not see that she has picked the skin around the nail until it shines dark and wet.== Her mother keeps interrupting to add practical details nobody asked for, the stipend, the commute, the fact that the firm has international branches, the fact that Silvia has finally started understanding how the world works, and every time she says this she gives the table that humble, suffering smile some mothers use when they want credit for having broken in a difficult horse. I look at Silvia’s shoulders going inward under that smile and feel the old righteous ache rise in me, the one that has never entirely left, the one that still believes young women are mostly ruined by committee.

![A young woman with a bloodied thumbnail stands in a cramped dining room after dinner while her elderly relative realizes that survival can look disturbingly like self-burial. In a humid, crowded family dining room, an old woman confronts the unsettling truth that a young woman has not been crushed by expectation so much as remade herself to outlive it.](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:4800/format:webp/0*MJ6FeyCkxRnEWxaj)

==This is not a theory I learned from books, though books helped decorate it. I learned it in a kitchen where my own mother corrected the way I laughed as if laughter had hem lengths and age limits==, and in a school corridor where a teacher once told me, with her upper lip beaded in sweat and a chalky stripe on her sari blouse, that ambition in a girl becomes vulgar when it has no visible destination. I learned it from watching brothers who could not locate the lid of the rice tin appoint themselves wardens of a sister’s reputation, and from ==watching women become suddenly dignified in the eyes of the family only after they had made themselves dull enough to be discussed approvingly over tea==. ==By nineteen, everyone around a girl develops an administrative interest in her future, which would be touching if it did not so often arrive with a quiet hunger to make that future legible, safe, marriageable, and above all easy to summarize to outsiders.==

After dinner the others drift toward the television where people are shouting over one another about elections or migration or cooking oils, I cannot tell which, because modern broadcasting has given the same nervous bloodstream to every subject. Silvia remains at the table folding and unfolding the corner of her napkin until it looks like something prayed over too often, and I stay with her because old women are especially susceptible to the fantasy that one well-timed sentence can still change a younger life. I lean toward her across the thickening gravy and the glasses ringed with fingerprints and tell her, in a voice I am sure sounds rough with intimacy, that this is how it starts, with respectable internships and sensible shoes and the gradual surrender of whatever once made a girl difficult to house. I tell her she must not let them use practicality to sand down the parts of her that were hers before salaries and family strategy entered the room, and even while I am saying it I can hear a stale sermon smell rising off my own words, because advice almost always reaches the young after it has already gone limp in the speaker’s hands.

I expect embarrassment, maybe tenderness, maybe that injured gratitude girls sometimes perform for old women because we are near the end and they wish to be decent to us, but Silvia only presses her thumb harder against the white tablecloth until a rusty half-moon stains the fabric near the bread basket, and then she looks up at me with an expression so cold and level that I feel the soft skin under my chin tighten. She tells me in a low voice, without drama and without any wish to be misunderstood, that nobody took the owl pellets from her, that she threw them into the building incinerator herself after deciding she was finished with hobbies that produced nothing but disgust and clutter. She says her mother did not force the internship either, that she chose it because money behaves better than love and better than family and certainly better than talent, and that by twenty-four she intends to be earning enough to rent a place where nobody can ask where she is going or why she came home late or whether she plans to waste her degree on feelings. Then she gives the smallest shrug, not stylish, not tragic, just efficient, and says that everybody keeps pretending girls are being murdered when in fact some of them are learning to do the cutting with a steadier hand.

In a humid, crowded family dining room, an old woman confronts the unsettling truth that a young woman has not been crushed by expectation so much as remade herself to outlive it.](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:4800/format:webp/0*9NC4nKCLWrs7jnlS)

==I wish I could tell you this was the moment I admired her, but what I felt first was shame, followed closely by irritation, because there is nothing more embarrassing than discovering that your beautiful theory about female suffering has been overheard by someone who finds it sentimental.== I had mistaken her for a victim because victimhood is easier for women of my generation to love than appetite sharpened into method, and because I had dressed up my own compromises for so many years in the language of duty that I still cannot look straight at a young woman who chooses calculation without feeling both disgusted and envious. Silvia was not shrinking to fit the room the way I had imagined while her mother discussed sodium and property tax and internships with the bright, dead cheerfulness of a woman inventorying canned goods. She was building something harder than shrinking, something with no softness in it at all, and I could not tell whether I was looking at survival or at another form of burial that happened to come with better shoes and a spreadsheet.

From the other room I could hear my son-in-law laugh with a wet, phlegmy satisfaction at something on television, and Silvia’s mother called out to ask whether anyone had seen the good container lids, which is the kind of question women use when they want to remind a household they are still the unpaid archivists of its daily life. Silvia turned her thumb over and examined the blood as if it belonged to somebody else, then asked whether I wanted tea, and her voice had gone flat again, flat in a way that no longer sounded oppressed to me, only managed. I said no, though what I wanted was to ask whether she ever missed the girl with the biscuit tin and the tiny bones laid out on paper towels to dry, and whether missing that girl would even strike her as weakness, and whether there comes an age when a woman cannot tell the difference anymore between protecting herself and helping the world finish the job. Instead, I sat there with the bent serving spoon and the radiator knocking in the wall like an old man trying not to cough, while Silvia carried the plates into the kitchen with the blood drying brown around her thumbnail, and I found that I no longer knew which of us the room had made more efficiently.
