I know that might feel a bit jarring, like it’s slowing you down, but it isn’t actually slowing down your learning—it’s only exposing the fact that your perception of learning does not accurately reflect actual learning. Really, it’s speeding up your actual learning, and the only thing it’s slowing down is your perception of learning.

Now, you might say “but I had learned so much, and I had it down pat, and then I forgot it all when I focused my effort on solving a problem.” But the thing is, if you can’t retrieve that information from memory at the snap of a finger, after thinking about other stuff or zooming in to focus on a specific problem, it means you didn’t really have it down pat.

# The Need to Practice Retrieving From Memory

If you want to actually retain information you consume, you need to practice retrieving it from memory, not just reconsuming it. You have retained information if and only if you can regenerate it from memory. So naturally that is the exercise that must be practiced.

The way to avoid this problem and maximize your learning is to switch over to active problem-solving immediately after consuming a minimum effective dose of information. That minimum dose is not “read until it feels comfortable.” It is just enough input to understand the basic vocabulary, the rules of the system, the shape of the problem, and maybe one or two examples of how an expert approaches it. After that, the highest-value move is to close the book, pause the video, hide the answer, and try to produce something from memory.

I know that might feel jarring, like it is slowing you down, but that feeling is misleading. Cognitive-science research has repeatedly found that the activities that feel smoothest in the moment—rereading, watching another explanation, highlighting, nodding along—often create a sense of fluency that is easy to mistake for real mastery. In a major review of learning techniques, Dunlosky and colleagues rated practice testing and distributed practice as “high utility,” while rereading and highlighting were rated much lower because they produce less reliable gains in performance. ([Western Kentucky University](https://www.wku.edu/senate/documents/improving_student_learning_dunlosky_2013.pdf "PPI453266.indd"))

This is the uncomfortable part: active problem-solving does not slow down learning. It slows down the illusion of learning. It forces your brain to confront the difference between “I recognize this when someone else explains it” and “I can reconstruct this myself when the explanation is gone.” Those are very different abilities. Recognition feels good because the material seems familiar. Retrieval feels harder because you have to rebuild the idea from the inside. But that rebuilding process is precisely what strengthens learning.

This is why researchers often describe retrieval practice as more than a measurement tool. A test does not merely reveal what you know; it can change what you know by strengthening the memory itself. In Roediger and Karpicke’s work on test-enhanced learning, students who restudied performed slightly better after five minutes, recalling 81% versus 75% for students who had practiced retrieval. But after delays, the pattern reversed: after two days, the retrieval-practice group recalled 68% versus 54%, and after one week, 56% versus 42%. In other words, rereading looked better immediately, but retrieval practice produced more durable learning. ([learninglab.psych.purdue.edu](https://learninglab.psych.purdue.edu/downloads/2006/2006_Roediger_Karpicke_PsychSci.pdf "untitled"))

That is the trap learners fall into. They judge learning by how easy the material feels while it is in front of them, not by whether they can use it later when the material is gone. But real learning has to survive delay, distraction, and transfer. You have not truly learned a concept merely because you understood it during the lecture. You have learned it when you can bring it back after thinking about something else, use it in a slightly unfamiliar problem, and explain why the answer works.

Now, you might say, “But I had learned so much, and I had it down pat, and then I forgot it all when I focused my effort on solving a problem.” But that is exactly the diagnostic value of problem-solving. If the knowledge disappears the moment you stop looking at the source material, then it was not yet available knowledge. It was supported knowledge. The book, video, notes, or worked solution were doing part of the cognitive work for you.

That does not mean you learned nothing. It means the knowledge was still fragile. It had been encoded enough to feel familiar, but not retrieved enough to become reliable. In Karpicke and Blunt’s study on science learning, retrieval practice produced better meaningful learning than elaborative studying with concept mapping, even on questions requiring comprehension and inference. Strikingly, 84% of students performed better after retrieval practice, but 75% believed concept mapping would be just as effective or more effective. Their perception of learning was almost exactly backwards.

This is why the discomfort matters. The moment you try to solve a problem and blank out, you have not failed—you have found the boundary of your learning. That blank space is useful information. It tells you what your brain cannot yet retrieve without assistance. And once you identify that gap, you can go back to the material with a sharper purpose. You are no longer passively consuming. You are hunting for the missing piece.

The need, then, is not simply to consume more information. The need is to practice retrieving information from memory. If you want to retain what you consume, you need to repeatedly ask your brain to regenerate it: define the idea without looking, solve a problem from scratch, explain the concept in your own words, draw the diagram from memory, derive the formula, predict the next step, or write down everything you remember before checking the source.

This is also why the “minimum effective dose” matters. You do need enough instruction to avoid flailing helplessly. Research on desirable difficulties makes this distinction clear: difficulty is useful when the learner has enough background knowledge to respond to the challenge; otherwise, the difficulty can become unproductive. ([bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu](https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2021/01/RABjorkELBjorkJARMAC2020ForPostingSingleSpaced.pdf "Microsoft Word - RABjork&ELBjorkJARMAC2020ForPostingSingleSpaced.docx")) So the goal is not to jump into problems with zero preparation. The goal is to avoid staying in preparation mode long after you have enough to begin.

A good rule is this: consume until you can attempt, then attempt until you can diagnose, then return to the material only to repair what the attempt exposed. That loop is far more powerful than simply consuming until you feel ready. Feeling ready often comes from familiarity. Actual readiness comes from retrieval.

You have retained information, in the practical sense, when you can regenerate it from memory under the conditions where you need to use it. Not perfectly, not instantly, and not with photographic precision—but reliably enough that the knowledge is available when the notes are closed and the problem is in front of you. So naturally, that is the exercise that must be practiced. Not rereading. Not rewatching. Not merely recognizing. Retrieving. Solving. Reconstructing. Producing.

That is where learning stops being a feeling and becomes a capability.
