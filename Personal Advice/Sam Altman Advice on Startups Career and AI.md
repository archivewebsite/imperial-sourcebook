# Every Piece of Advice from Sam Altman

---

## About This Document

Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI and former president of Y Combinator, has published a large body of advice across his blog, the YC Startup Playbook, essays, interviews, and talks. This document merges two prior compilations into one structured reference, paraphrasing the advice into plain language while preserving the core ideas.

The advice is grouped into the following sections:

1. High-Level Framework for Success  
2. Startup Advice

# 1. High-Level Framework for Success

## 1. Compound Yourself

- Think of your career like compound interest.
- Aim for an exponential trajectory, not a linear one.
- Choose work where each unit of effort produces increasing returns over time.
- Look for leverage through capital, technology, brand, network effects, and managing people.
- Avoid fields where two years of experience is basically the same as twenty.
- Keep your rate of learning high.
- Let small opportunities go so you can focus on potential step changes.
- Take time between projects to find something that could make the rest of your career a footnote.
- Trust the exponential curve. Be patient.

## 2. Have Almost Too Much Self-Belief

- Believe in yourself almost to the point of delusion.
- Cultivate self-belief early.
- As your judgment proves out, trust yourself more.
- Without self-belief, you will not pursue contrarian ideas.
- Most large amounts of value come from being right about something important when others think you are wrong.
- Balance self-belief with self-awareness.
- Listen to criticism as if it might be true, then decide whether to act on it.
- Truth-seeking separates confidence from delusion.

## 3. Think Independently

- Original thinking is hard to teach and often punished in school.
- Practice first-principles thinking.
- Generate your own ideas.
- Surround yourself with people who can challenge and improve your ideas.
- Find fast, cheap ways to test ideas in the real world.
- Learn to operate in uncertain situations.
- Expect to fail many times and be really right once.
- Give yourself many chances to get lucky.

## 4. Get Good at Sales

- All great careers eventually become sales jobs in some form.
- You need to convince customers, employees, investors, partners, the press, and others.
- Communication, especially writing, is critical.
- Make your thinking clear first; then use plain, concise language.
- The best way to sell is to genuinely believe in what you are selling.
- Sales is a learnable skill.
- Show up in person when the conversation is important.

## 5. Make It Easy to Take Risks

- Most people overestimate risk and underestimate reward.
- Take risks early, when you have less to lose.
- Structure your life so that you can take bets.
- Look for situations where you lose 1x if wrong but gain 100x if right.
- Keep your life cheap and flexible.
- Avoid lifestyle inflation that traps you in predictable work.
- The real risk is often not doing something you will later regret.

## 6. Focus

- Focus is a force multiplier.
- Spend more time deciding what to focus on.
- Working on the right thing matters more than working many hours.
- Once you know what matters, execute relentlessly.
- Say no to almost everything else.
- Slow-moving people rarely produce outlier results.

## 7. Work Hard

- Working smart or hard can get you far; doing both is required for the 99th percentile.
- Extreme people get extreme results.
- Momentum compounds.
- Success creates more success.
- Work hard early in your career because effort compounds.
- Work on things you care about with people you enjoy to avoid burnout.
- Working very hard is not mandatory for everyone, but it is usually required for outlier success.

## 8. Be Bold

- It is often easier to do a hard startup than an easy one.
- People want to join missions that matter.
- Ambitious work attracts help.
- Let yourself become more ambitious.
- Work on what you actually want to work on.
- Follow your curiosity.

## 9. Be Willful

- You can bend the world to your will more often than most people think.
- Most people never try hard enough.
- Ask for what you want.
- You will be rejected often, but when asking works, it can work spectacularly.
- Persist long enough for luck to matter.
- Optimism is necessary for willfulness.

## 10. Be Hard to Compete With

- If what you do can easily be done by someone else, it eventually will be done cheaper.
- Build durable leverage.
- Combine skills from multiple fields.
- Develop strong relationships.
- Build a personal brand based on real substance.
- Avoid copying what everyone around you is doing.

## 11. Build a Network

- Great work usually requires teams.
- Your network of talented collaborators can become the limit of what you accomplish.
- Help people with no immediate expectation of return.
- Be generous with upside.
- Take good care of people who work with you.
- Learn what people are unusually good at and help them do that.
- Look for undiscovered talent by rate of improvement, not just current accomplishment.
- Spend time with positive people who support your ambitions.

## 12. Own Things

- Almost no one gets truly rich from salary alone.
- Wealth comes from owning things that increase rapidly in value.
- Ownership can mean equity in a company, real estate, intellectual property, or another scalable asset.
- Selling time scales linearly.
- Equity can scale exponentially.
- The best way to create valuable ownership is to make something people want at scale.

## 13. Be Internally Driven

- Most people are externally driven: they work to impress others.
- External motivation leads to consensus career paths and consensus ideas.
- It also causes people to miscalculate risk.
- The most successful people are internally driven.
- They do excellent work because they feel compelled to do it.
- After money and status stop motivating you, internal drive is what remains.
- Start defining success as doing excellent work in areas important to you.

# 2. Startup Advice

## Core Startup Principles

- Make something people want.
- Build something a small number of users love rather than something many users merely like.
- Have both a great team and a great market.
- Work on a market that is growing fast or about to grow fast.
- Prefer ideas that seem bad but are actually good.
- Be contrarian and right, not merely contrarian.
- Start with an idea or project, not with the abstract desire to have a company.
- Do not start a startup unless you are willing to work on it for ten years.
- Do not start a company just because you want to be a founder.
- Be ready for the company to take over your life.

## What Founders Should Spend Time On

- Write code.
- Talk to users.
- Build the company.
- Most other activities are distractions.
- Be suspicious of work that is not building product, talking to users, selling, or growing.
- Avoid “movie version of a startup” activities: conferences, endless coffee meetings, excessive advisor meetings, and over-lawyering.
- Do things that do not scale early.
- Recruit users manually.
- Watch users use your product.
- Founder-led sales and customer support are valuable for as long as possible.

## Product

- Obsess about product quality.
- A great product is the foundation of long-term growth.
- Growth hacks stop working eventually; great products keep working.
- Launch earlier than feels comfortable.
- Start with the simplest possible version.
- Iterate weekly.
- Talk to users, improve, talk again, and repeat.
- Product includes every interaction: support, sales, onboarding, and the full user experience.
- Ask whether users would be upset if your product disappeared.
- Do not deceive yourself about whether users actually love the product.

## Growth

- Growth solves nearly all startup problems.
- Lack of growth is usually solved only by growth.
- Momentum is critical; do not lose it.
- Pick a single important growth metric.
- The company builds what the CEO measures.
- Beware vanity metrics.
- Retention matters as much as acquisition.
- Ask customers where to find more people like them.
- Generate revenue early.
- Do not forget to make money.
- Spending more to acquire users than their lifetime value is not a strategy.
- Be suspicious of buying users before you understand retention and economics.

## Focus and Execution

- Focus intensely.
- Say no often.
- Do not do too many things at once.
- No great company starts by doing many things simultaneously.
- Move fast.
- Speed is one of a startup’s main advantages over large companies.
- Better to make a decision and be wrong than to equivocate.
- Once you switch from project mode to company mode, be decisive and act quickly.
- Think in hours, not weeks.
- Execute with intensity.
- Do what it takes and do not make excuses.
- You are more likely to die from bad execution than from competitors.

## Strategy

- Have a strategy; most people do not.
- Occasionally step back and ask where you are going.
- Growth toward a valuable destination matters.
- A company eventually needs a durable advantage or monopoly-like position.
- Be rigid about the mission and flexible about almost everything else.
- If you pivot, pivot fully and with conviction.
- The worst pivot is keeping one foot in the old idea and one foot in the new one.
- Startups should require as few miracles as possible, but at least one.

## Competition

- Competitors are usually a distraction.
- Most startups die from suicide, not murder.
- Ignore competitors unless they beat you with a real shipped product.
- Press releases are easier than code; code is easier than building a great product.
- Focus on making your own business better.

## Money and Burn

- Be frugal.
- Keep burn low until you know things are working.
- Keep your personal burn rate low.
- Watch cash obsessively.
- Do not run out of money.
- Raise a little more money than you think you need.
- Too much money can make you undisciplined.
- Get to ramen profitability as quickly as possible if you can.
- Have a plan that does not depend on raising money.

## Fundraising

- Raise when you need money or when it is available on good terms.
- The best fundraising tactic is having a good company.
- Investors want companies that can become huge.
- Run fundraising processes in parallel, not sequentially.
- Anything other than “yes” usually means “no.”
- When investors say no, believe the no but not necessarily the reason.
- Raise on promise and clean terms.
- Do not over-optimize valuation.
- Good investors can be worth a reasonable premium.
- Reference-check investors by asking founders how they behave when things go badly.
- Prefer a few highly involved investors over many lightly engaged ones.
- Give investors specific ways to help.

## Boards

- The board’s role is advice and consent.
- The CEO should set strategy, not outsource it to the board.
- Founders should generally keep board control in the first round.
- Board observers are usually a headache.
- Great board members can be valuable outside forcing functions.

## Culture

- Over-communicate with your team.
- Transparency is your friend.
- Share financials and key metrics with the company regularly.
- Build a culture that rewards output.
- Praise good work.
- Celebrate wins.
- Lead by example.
- Keep the organization as flat as possible.
- Define mission and values early because they tend to stick.
- Culture is defined by who you hire, fire, and promote.
- Early culture should have strong shared values while preserving diversity of thought.

## CEO Mindset

- The CEO’s job is to make sure the company wins.
- Core CEO duties: set vision and strategy, evangelize, hire and manage, raise money, and set the quality bar for execution.
- The CEO does not get to make excuses.
- Be externally optimistic when everything is going wrong.
- Privately stay paranoid about what could break.
- Everything will feel broken all the time.
- Fix it with a smile and reassure the team.
- Being CEO is often miserable, but when it is good, it is very good.
- Build relationships with peer CEOs who understand the role.
- On bad days, remember that tomorrow may be better.
- Eat well, sleep well, exercise, and maintain close relationships.

# 3. The Startup Playbook

## Great Idea

- Know what you are building and why.
- The idea should be clear, concise, and emotionally exciting to at least some people.
- The best startup ideas often initially sound bad.
- A great idea should be new, not derivative.
- “10x better” can count as new.
- Prefer a fast-growing market to a large but stagnant market.
- Major technological shifts create opportunities because incumbents are bad at responding.
- It is often best to own a large part of a small market first.
- Do not be too secretive about your idea.
- People with good ideas are common; people willing to execute are rare.
- Do not force ideas.
- Learn widely, notice problems, watch technological shifts, work on interesting projects, and spend time with smart people.

## Great Team

- Mediocre teams do not build great companies.
- Founder traits that matter: unstoppability, determination, formidability, resourcefulness, intelligence, and passion.
- These traits matter more than experience.
- A technology startup should have at least one founder who can build and at least one founder who can sell or talk to users.
- Choose cofounders carefully.
- Do not pick a cofounder you just met.
- Cofounder breakups are a major startup killer.
- Best case: a good cofounder.
- Next best: solo founder.
- Worst case: a bad cofounder.
- Set equity early; nearly equal is often best.

## Great Product

- Build a product-improvement engine.
- Talk to users.
- Watch users use the product.
- Find what is bad.
- Make it better.
- Repeat quickly.
- Break work into small pieces.
- Do not batch everything into giant launches.
- Simplicity is always good.
- Care about details.
- When stuck, talk to users.

## Great Execution

- Growth and momentum are the keys to execution.
- Never lose momentum.
- Establish a drumbeat of progress: product releases, customers, hires, partnerships, and milestones.
- Set aggressive but achievable goals.
- Review goals regularly.
- Keep a running list of what blocks growth.
- Ask whether each activity is the best way to optimize growth.
- Do not worry too early about massive scale.
- Think about what happens at 10x current scale, not 1,000x.
- When something works, keep going.

# 4. Hiring and Managing

## Hiring Philosophy

- After vision and product-market fit, hiring is one of the highest-leverage things a founder does.
- Spend more time hiring than feels natural.
- In recruiting mode, hiring may take 25% to 50% of your time.
- The first hiring advice is: do not hire unless you must.
- Companies often work better when they are smaller.
- Employees add cost, complexity, communication overhead, and inertia.
- Do not tie your self-worth to headcount.

## Who to Hire

- Hire smart, effective people.
- Hire people committed to what you are doing.
- Value aptitude over experience for most roles.
- Look for raw intelligence and a track record of getting things done.
- Hire people you like.
- Avoid chronically negative people.
- Avoid people who focus too much on title or org size.
- Look for people who could start their own companies.
- Hire people you would describe as unusually driven.
- Do not compromise on quality.
- One bad hire can seriously damage a company.

## How to Source Candidates

- The best source is friends and friends of friends.
- When you find a star, ask them for the names of everyone else you should hire.
- Great people are rarely actively looking.
- You often need to recruit or poach them.
- Use investors and their networks.
- Mention hiring needs in investor updates.
- Do not rely too heavily on job boards.
- Treat sourcing as a long-term investment.

## How to Interview

- Learn a role yourself before hiring for it when possible.
- Ask candidates about specific projects.
- Go deep on what they personally did.
- Ask how they spend their time and what they accomplished recently.
- Have candidates audition for roles with real paid work when possible.
- Make interviewers commit to hire/no-hire decisions.
- Write feedback before group discussion.
- Teach your team how to interview.
- Keep the process organized.
- Make sure candidates leave with a positive impression even if you do not hire them.

## Compensation

- Be generous with equity.
- Keep salaries reasonable but give meaningful upside.
- For early hires, consider giving more equity than investors suggest.
- You want people motivated by the upside, not just salary.
- Offer letters can include scenarios showing what equity might be worth.

## Firing

- Fire quickly when it is clearly not working.
- First-time founders almost always wait too long.
- Fire toxic people even if they are good at their job.
- Everyone usually knows when someone is not working out before the founder acts.

## Managing

- Learn to manage.
- Find mentors who can teach management.
- Books help less than experienced people.
- Make sure employees are happy, challenged, and growing.
- Hold regular all-hands or staff meetings.
- Keep people informed about progress and metrics.
- Give people responsibility and trust.
- Do not go into hero mode and try to do everything yourself.
- Push people enough that they exceed their own expectations, but not so hard that they burn out.
- Keep people by giving them new challenges and momentum.

## Remote Work and Office

- In the earliest days, being in the same place helps culture form.
- Avoid remote employees before culture is strong if possible.
- For very small companies, an apartment or house can be the right office.
- The right environment should support focus, communication, and speed.

# 5. Productivity

## Work on the Right Things

- Picking the right thing matters more than moving quickly.
- Productivity in the wrong direction is not worth much.
- You cannot be highly productive on things you do not care about.
- Try to find work you would do for free.
- If you dislike your work for a long time, consider a major change.
- Trust that you can learn almost anything quickly.

## Prioritization

- The three pillars: do important things, avoid stupid things, and make lists.
- Make yearly, monthly, and daily lists.
- Use lists to force prioritization.
- Every day, identify the one to three most important things.
- Do those first.
- Keep a daily to-do list with one or two big things at the top.
- Prioritize for momentum.
- Start and end each day with something you can make progress on.
- Be ruthless about saying no.
- Do non-critical things in the fastest acceptable way.
- Avoid productivity systems that become a substitute for real work.

## Time Management

- Most people do not value their own time enough.
- Avoid unnecessary meetings.
- Most meetings should be shorter than the default one-hour slot, or long enough for deep work.
- Use different times of day for different kinds of work.
- Switch tasks when attention fades.
- Leave open space to think.
- Books, conversations, and time in nature can help clarify what to work on.

## Physical Factors

- Sleep is the most important productivity factor.
- Exercise is second.
- Eat well.
- Avoid things that harm sleep.
- Keep your workspace comfortable, quiet, naturally lit, and interruption-free when possible.
- Do not neglect family, friends, or things that clear your head.
- Sacrificing close relationships for productivity is usually a bad trade.

## Tools and Systems

- Write custom software for repetitive annoying tasks when useful.
- Learn to type fast.
- Master keyboard shortcuts.
- Build systems that reduce cognitive load.
- Avoid letting unwanted tasks pile up and consume mental energy.
- Slight overcommitment can be useful; massive overcommitment is disastrous.

# 6. Idea Generation

- Idea generation is one of the most important founder skills.
- Giving founders ideas usually does not work.
- Good founders have ideas about many things.
- If you cannot generate startup ideas, work on idea generation first.
- Put yourself in the right environment.
- Spend time with people who have a feel for the future.
- Look for people who entertain improbable plans, are optimistic, are creatively smart, and have high idea flux.
- Avoid people who belittle ambition.
- The best ideas are fragile and often sound silly at first.
- Create an environment where bad ideas can be spoken out loud.
- Project yourself 20 years into the future and think backward.
- Identify major tectonic shifts.
- Ask what is newly possible this year that was not possible last year.
- Distinguish real trends from fake trends:
  - Real trend: a small number of people use something intensely.
  - Fake trend: many people use something casually.
- A strong signal is being able to say: “This is going to happen; I just do not know if we will be the ones to do it.”
- Ask whether the idea could become huge if it worked.
- Think about why your idea might compound at scale.
- Consider founder-company fit.
- Ask whether you can explain why most people think it is a bad idea and why they are wrong.

# 7. Founder Traits and Mindset

## Traits to Look For

- Obsession.
- Focus.
- Frugality.
- Love for the problem or users.
- Intelligence.
- Communication skill.
- Execution speed.
- Rate of improvement.
- Deep motivation.
- Resourcefulness.
- Determination.
- Formidability.
- Passion.

## Execution

- Execution speed is strongly correlated with success.
- Founders who do not execute quickly rarely succeed.
- More people have good ideas than are willing to do the work.
- Be a doer, not a talker.
- Sometimes sheer force of will works.
- All startups are broken in at least one major way; keep going.

## Motivation

- Starting a startup just to get rich usually does not work.
- The pain is too high unless the mission matters.
- Founders need deep motivation.
- Missionaries usually outperform mercenaries.
- Sustain long-term work only on something you believe is important and actually enjoy.

## Toughness

- Become formidable.
- Become tough.
- The road will make you doubt yourself.
- Learn to stay optimistic externally when things are melting down.
- Do not let yourself get pushed around.
- Confident is good; arrogant is bad.

# 8. Career and People Advice

- Choose work at the intersection of what you are good at, what you enjoy, and what creates value for the world.
- If you cannot find that intersection, it is hard to have large impact.
- Find your tribe.
- Move toward clusters of people doing great work in your field.
- Personal connections matter enormously.
- Help people with no immediate expectation of benefit.
- Decide when to quit based on your own judgment, not just external criticism.
- If users are not using your product, pay attention, but do not automatically quit.
- Quit when you have run out of ideas or no longer believe the path is working.
- Being young, poor, and unknown can be a gift for taking risks.
- Do not overrate reputational damage from failure.
- Have strong opinions but be flexible on details.
- Refinement is different from random pivoting.
- Learn from people who have done it before, but do not blindly follow old advice.
- Beware of distractions after early success.
- Think bigger; ambitious missions make recruiting and attention easier.
- Find driven peers, investors, employees, and mentors.

# 9. Life Advice

## Relationships

- Put family, friends, and romantic partners high on your priority list.
- Prefer a few deep friendships over many shallow ones.
- Do not lose touch with old friends.
- Stay up late talking with people sometimes.
- Throw parties.
- Tell your parents you love them.
- Visit home when you can.
- Love your partner intensely.
- Be nice to strangers.
- Be nice when it does not matter.
- Go out of your way to help people.
- Forgive people.
- Do not screw people.
- Do not burn bridges.
- Pick your battles carefully.

## Time and Mortality

- Life is not a dress rehearsal.
- This is probably it.
- Time is extremely limited and passes quickly.
- The days are long but the decades are short.
- Do not waste your youth.
- Money cannot buy back time.
- If you think you will regret not doing something, you should probably do it.
- Most people regret inaction more than action.
- Do new things often; it slows the perception of time and keeps life interesting.
- Aim to do something big, new, and risky every year.

## Happiness and Fulfillment

- Do what makes you happy and fulfilled.
- Do not do things only because others want you to.
- Cut negative people out of your life.
- Do not maintain relationships with people you dislike.
- Do not chase status.
- Status without substance is hollow.
- Be grateful.
- Keep problems in perspective.
- Do not complain too much.
- Do not hate other people’s success.
- Some people will hate your success; ignore it.

## Work and Ambition

- It is hard to do great work on something you do not care about.
- It is hard to be happy if you dislike your work.
- Work very hard, but not so hard that the rest of life passes by.
- Aim to be the best in the world at what you do.
- Even if you fall short, you will likely end up somewhere good.
- Take career risks early.
- Think hard about what you like and what fields will become important.
- Talk to people in those fields.

## Money

- Money does not buy happiness, but it buys freedom.
- Lack of money is stressful.
- Having enough not to worry about rent matters more than having enough for luxuries.
- Making money is often more fun than spending it.
- Money spent on friends, experiences, saving time, travel, and causes you believe in is rarely regretted.
- Keep your personal burn rate low because it creates options.

## Learning and Media

- Learn voraciously.
- Talk to people more.
- Read more long-form content.
- Read fewer tweets.
- Watch less TV.
- Spend less time online.
- Build your own productivity system.

## Health

- Exercise.
- Eat well.
- Sleep.
- Spend time in nature regularly.
- Most things are fine in moderation; almost nothing is fine in extreme amounts.

## Emotional Perspective

- Do not worry so much.
- Things are rarely as risky as they seem.
- Most advice is biased toward conservative paths.
- This too shall pass.
- Existential angst is part of life.
- It is especially common around major life or career milestones.
- Nothing is wrong with you for feeling it.
- Given enough time, humans can adjust to almost anything.
- Pause before acting.
- Pause longer if angry.
- Do not judge others too quickly.
- You do not know their whole story.
- Be empathetic.

# 10. Investing in Startups

- To invest well, you need access to good deals, good judgment, and the ability to make founders choose you.
- Make it your job to help people who may become future deal flow.
- Help founders with candidates, fundraising, customer introductions, and general advice.
- Build a brand through long-form writing and real insight.
- Great founders are the key.
- Learn to identify great founders before they are obvious.
- The market rewards finding great but inexperienced people.
- Look for companies with:
  - Compelling founders.
  - A mission that attracts talent.
  - A product so good users tell friends.
  - A fast-growing market.
  - Network effects or low marginal costs.
  - Ability to grow quickly.
  - A fundamentally new or 10x-better product.
- Limit yourself to opportunities that could become enormous.
- Power laws dominate startup investing.
- Failures matter less than missing the giant winners.
- When founders ask for help, they often need emotional support as much as tactical advice.
- Be a friend when things are hard.

# 11. AI-Era and Education Advice

- The AI era may be an unusually exciting time to start a career.
- Get very good at using AI tools.
- AI fluency today may be analogous to coding fluency in earlier eras.
- Learn how to learn.
- Cultivate adaptability.
- Cultivate resilience.
- Creativity remains important.
- Coding still matters because it teaches computational thinking.
- The number of people who need to think computationally may increase even if manual coding decreases.
- Asking great questions may become more valuable than raw knowledge.
- Figuring out what to ask can matter more than figuring out the answer.
- Traditional career advice may work less well in a fast-changing world.
- Young people should develop their own intuitions about the future.
- Most things are not one-way doors.
- Try things; if they do not work, adjust and try something else.
- The risky thing is often failing to try things that might really work.
- AI may enable very small teams, possibly even one-person companies, to create enormous value.

# 12. Non-Technical Founders, Secrecy, and Execution

## Non-Technical Founders

- If you are a solo non-technical founder, learn to hack.
- You can often become good enough in a few months to build a useful first version.
- A rough version that gets user feedback is better than waiting indefinitely for a technical cofounder.
- Do not hide behind being “the idea person.”
- If you are not willing to learn to code, remember that startups contain much harder challenges.
- Meeting a stranger solely to become cofounders rarely works.
- It is better to work with someone you already know and have worked with.
- Learning to build yourself can be faster than finding the perfect cofounder.

## Secrecy

- Do not overestimate how much others care about your idea.
- Even if a large company sees your plan, execution is still hard.
- Ideas are abundant; disciplined execution creates value.
- Extreme secrecy is usually a negative signal.
- Being open about the broad vision helps you get feedback, attract talent, and learn from customers.
- Information alone is rarely a competitive advantage.
- Execution is the advantage.

3. Choose a fast-growing market.
4. Build a product users love.
5. Talk to users constantly.
6. Move fast.
7. Focus intensely.
8. Keep burn low.
9. Hire exceptional people slowly.
10. Fire quickly when necessary.
11. Build a strong culture.
12. Own equity.
13. Take risks early.
14. Think independently.
15. Believe in yourself.
16. Ask for what you want.
17. Help people generously.
18. Learn constantly.
19. Use new tools well.
20. Keep going for a very long time.
