The room was silent, but the air felt heavy. As Board President for Prescott Meals on Wheels, I was staring at a set of logistical numbers that didn’t add up. ==A sudden spike in food costs, a delivery van with a blown transmission, and a volunteer shortage — all hitting on the same Wednesday morning.==

For about four minutes, I felt the familiar “grip.” My chest tightened, my heart rate began its frantic climb, and my mind started racing toward a catastrophic future where we were failing the very people who depend on us for their only hot meal of the day.

That was the **stressor**. It was unavoidable. But in that moment of turmoil, I caught myself. I ==realized that while the van was broken,== ==*I*== ==didn’t have to be==. I took a breath, stepped back from the “story” I was telling myself, and remembered a 2,000-year-old secret.

By the time I sat down for the meeting, the problems remained, but the “feeling of being stressed” was gone. I wasn’t panicked; I was prepared.

So, do I ever get stressed Of course. This said, I’m so much calmer now that the more I practice my mental response to stress, the more I’m sure it would be the same for you.

## The Stoic Shield: The Dichotomy of Control

The Roman Emperor Marcus Aurelius woke up every morning to a plague, a crumbling empire, and the threat of betrayal. His journals — what we now call *Meditations* — weren’t written for an audience; they were a survival manual.

He knew a secret that modern science is only now proving: **External events have no power to touch the soul.**

We live in an era of “perpetual notification.” Our nervous systems are being hijacked by global crises, local demands, and the digital hum of our pockets. But there is a fundamental difference between **stress** (the biological pressure) and **distress** (our psychological reaction).

One is a biological reality; the other is a story we can choose to stop telling. The foundation of Stoicism is a deceptively simple question: *Is this within my control*

Epictetus, a former slave turned philosopher, taught that our only true domain is our **prohairesis** — our reasoned choice. When we “feel stressed,” it is almost always because we are trying to exert power over things outside that domain: other people’s opinions, the economy, or the weather.

> ==*“People are disturbed not by things, but by the view which they take of them.” — Epictetus*==

## The Neuroscience of “The Gap.”

Modern research in **Cognitive Appraisal Theory** mirrors this ancient wisdom. Dr. Viktor Frankl, a psychiatrist and Holocaust survivor, famously noted that between stimulus and response, there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response.

Neurologically, this is a tug-of-war between your amygdala (the emotional alarm system) and your prefrontal cortex (the executive control center).

When a stressor hits — a harsh email, a missed flight — the amygdala triggers the “fight or flight” response. However, research shows that ==**Affective Labeling**== ==(simply naming the emotion or the fact) shifts brain activity from the amygdala to the prefrontal cortex.==

- **Stress:** “I’m going to lose my job; this is a disaster!” (Amygdala hijacking).
- **Response:** “I have received a critical email. I will address point A and B.” (Prefrontal mastery).

## The Biological “Check Engine” Light

Stress is nothing more than information. It is your body’s way of saying, *“Pay attention; something is happening.”* According to the **Yerkes-Dodson Law**, a certain level of stress (eustress) actually improves performance. It sharpens focus and increases energy.

The goal isn’t to live a life devoid of stress — that’s called being dead. The goal is to prevent stress from turning into chronic distress.

> ==***The Secret:***== ==*Treat stress like a check engine light. You don’t scream at the light; you look under the hood.*==

## Practical Strategies for the “Unstressed” Life

==If you want to break the cycle of chronic “stressed-out” living, you need a toolkit that integrates Stoic discipline with neuroscience strategies.==

- **The Physiological Sigh:** To instantly “hack” your nervous system, use a technique popularized by Stanford neurobiologist Dr. Andrew Huberman: Take a deep inhale through the nose, follow it with a second, shorter “pop” inhale, and release a long, slow exhale through the mouth.
- **The “View from Above”:** When a problem feels overwhelming, zoom out. Visualize your city, the planet, and the galaxy. In the grand timeline of the universe, this “urgent” deadline is a speck of dust. This is about **perspective.**
- **Pre-Mortem (Premeditatio Malorum):** The Stoics practiced “the premeditation of evils.” They spent time imagining what could go wrong. Research on “mental contrasting” shows that visualizing obstacles makes us more likely to overcome them calmly when they arise.

## The Bottom Line

You cannot control the world. People will be late, markets will shift, and coffee will spill. Stress is a guaranteed part of the human experience.

But **being stressed** That is an internal state that requires your consent. As Marcus Aurelius wrote: *“Today I escaped anxiety. Or no, I discarded it, because it was within me, in my own perceptions — not outside.”*

==You are not the storm. You are the mountain. The storm happens== ==*around*== ==the mountain, but the mountain remains.==
