*Based on "Secrets of Mental Math" by Arthur Benjamin and Michael Shermer*

## Chapter 0: Quick Tricks — Easy (and Impressive) Calculations

### 1. Instant Multiplication by 11

To multiply any two-digit number by 11, add the two digits together and place the sum between the original digits.

**Example:** 32 × 11 → 3 + 2 = 5 → place 5 between 3 and 2 → **352**

If the digit sum exceeds 9, carry the 1 into the first digit.

**Example:** 85 × 11 → 8 + 5 = 13 → place 3 between digits, carry 1 → 8 becomes 9 → **935**

---

### 2. Squaring Two-Digit Numbers That End in 5

To square a number ending in 5: multiply the first digit by the next higher digit, then append 25.

**Example:** 35² → 3 × 4 = 12, append 25 → **1225**

**Example:** 85² → 8 × 9 = 72, append 25 → **7225**

---

### 3. Multiplying Two Two-Digit Numbers with the Same First Digit and Last Digits That Add Up to 10

When two numbers share the same first digit and their last digits sum to 10: multiply the first digit by the next higher digit, then append the product of the last digits.

**Example:** 83 × 87 → 8 × 9 = 72, 3 × 7 = 21 → **7221**

**Example:** 26 × 24 → 2 × 3 = 6, 6 × 4 = 24 → **624**

---

### 4. Subtraction Trick Using Rounding and Correction

Instead of subtracting a "messy" number, round it up to a convenient number, subtract, then add back the difference.

**Example:** 1241 − 587 → Round 587 up to 600. 1241 − 600 = 641. You subtracted 13 too much, so add back: 641 + 13 = **654**

---

### 5. Quickly Adding Certain Number Sequences

When you have a sequence where each number is the sum of the two preceding numbers (a Fibonacci-like sequence), the sum of ten such numbers equals 11 times the seventh number.

**Example:** Starting with 9 and 2: 9, 2, 11, 13, 24, 37, 61, 98, 159, 257. The seventh number is 61. Sum = 61 × 11 = **671**. (You can verify: 9 + 2 + 11 + 13 + 24 + 37 + 61 + 98 + 159 + 257 = 671.)

---

### 6. Quick Division to Produce Initial Decimal Digits

For the ratio of two consecutive numbers in a Fibonacci-like sequence, the quotient is approximately 1.618 (the golden ratio).

**Example:** 359 ÷ 222 = **1.61** (first three digits). This ratio is approximately the same regardless of the starting numbers in the sequence.

---

### 7. Calculating Restaurant Tips, Especially 15%

To calculate a 15% tip: find 10% of the bill, then cut that in half for 5%, and add them together.

**Example:** Bill = $42. 10% = $4.20. Half of $4.20 = $2.10. Tip = $4.20 + $2.10 = **$6.30**

---

### 8. Basics of Memorizing Numbers

Numbers can be memorized by converting digits to consonant sounds using a phonetic code, then forming words from those sounds: 1=t/d, 2=n, 3=m, 4=r, 5=l, 6=j/ch/sh, 7=k/g, 8=f/v, 9=p/b, 0=s/z.

**Example:** To remember the number 32, convert to consonants m-n, then form a word like "moon." To remember 47, convert to r-k, forming a word like "rock."

---

### 9. Determining the Day of the Week for a Given Date (Simple Version)

For any year in the 21st century: take the last two digits of the year as a dollar amount, add a 25% tip (ignoring cents), add the result, then subtract the largest multiple of 7. The remainder gives the day (1=Mon, 2=Tue, ... 7 or 0=Sun). In leap years, subtract $1 from the tip.

**Example:** January 1, 2030 → Bill: $30, Tip: half of 30 = 15, half of 15 = 7.50, keep the change = $7. Total = 37. Subtract 35 (multiple of 7) → 2 = **Tuesday**

## Chapter 1: Mental Addition and Subtraction

### 1. Left-to-Right Addition

Instead of working right-to-left as you do on paper, work left-to-right. Add the largest place values first, simplifying as you go.

**Example:** 47 + 32 → add 30 first: 47 + 30 = 77, then add 2: 77 + 2 = **79**

---

### 2. Two-Digit Addition

Break the second number into its tens and ones, then add each part from left to right.

**Example:** 67 + 28 → 67 + 20 = 87, then 87 + 8 = **95**

**Example:** 84 + 57 → 84 + 50 = 134, then 134 + 7 = **141**

---

### 3. Three-Digit Addition

Extend the left-to-right method to three digits, adding hundreds, then tens, then ones.

**Example:** 538 + 327 → 538 + 300 = 838, 838 + 20 = 858, 858 + 7 = **865**

---

### 4. Adding Three-Digit and Four-Digit Numbers

For a four-digit number plus a three-digit number, add the hundreds first, noting where digits overlap.

**Example:** 2700 + 567 → 27 hundred + 5 hundred = 32 hundred, attach 67 → **3267**

**Example:** 4560 + 171 → 4560 + 100 = 4660, then 4660 + 71 = **4731**

---

### 5. Left-to-Right Subtraction

Subtract the tens of the second number first, then the ones, simplifying from left to right.

**Example:** 86 − 25 → 86 − 20 = 66, then 66 − 5 = **61**

---

### 6. Two-Digit Subtraction

When no borrowing is needed, subtract digit by digit from left to right.

**Example:** 86 − 25 → 86 − 20 = 66, 66 − 5 = **61**

---

### 7. Subtraction by Rounding Up

If subtraction would require borrowing, round the second number up to the next multiple of 10, subtract, then add back the difference.

**Example:** 54 − 28 → Round 28 up to 30. 54 − 30 = 24. Add back 2: 24 + 2 = **26**

**Example:** 81 − 37 → Round 37 up to 40. 81 − 40 = 41. Add back 3: 41 + 3 = **44**

---

### 8. Three-Digit Subtraction

Apply the same strategies to three-digit problems. For numbers near a multiple of 100, round up and add back.

**Example:** 747 − 598 → 747 − 600 = 147, add back 2 → **149**

**Example:** 958 − 417 → 958 − 400 = 558, 558 − 10 = 548, 548 − 7 = **541**

---

### 9. Complement Techniques to Simplify Subtraction

The complement of a two-digit number is what you add to reach 100. The first digits sum to 9, the last digits sum to 10. Use complements to convert hard subtractions into easy additions.

**Example:** The complement of 68 is 32 (since 6+3=9, 8+2=10). So for 725 − 468: subtract 500 from 725 = 225, then add the complement of 68 (which is 32): 225 + 32 = **257**

## Chapter 2: Basic Multiplication

### 1. Multiplying 2-Digit × 1-Digit Numbers

Break the two-digit number into tens and ones, multiply each by the single digit, and add.

**Example:** 42 × 7 → (40 × 7) + (2 × 7) = 280 + 14 = **294**

---

### 2. Multiplication Using the Split-Number Method

Split the larger number into a multiple of 10 plus the remainder, multiply separately, and add.

**Example:** 48 × 4 → (40 × 4) + (8 × 4) = 160 + 32 = **192**

---

### 3. Multiplication by Rounding Up

When a number ends in 8 or 9, round it up, multiply, then subtract the excess.

**Example:** 69 × 6 → Round 69 to 70. 70 × 6 = 420. Subtract 1 × 6 = 6. Answer: 420 − 6 = **414**

**Example:** 78 × 9 → Round 78 to 80. 80 × 9 = 720. Subtract 2 × 9 = 18. Answer: 720 − 18 = **702**

---

### 4. Multiplying 3-Digit × 1-Digit Numbers

Break the three-digit number into hundreds, tens, and ones; multiply each by the single digit and add from left to right.

**Example:** 326 × 7 → (300 × 7) + (20 × 7) + (6 × 7) = 2100 + 140 + 42 = **2282**

**Example:** 647 × 4 → (600 × 4) + (40 × 4) + (7 × 4) = 2400 + 160 + 28 = **2588**

---

### 5. Squaring Two-Digit Numbers

To square any two-digit number, round up or down to the nearest multiple of 10. Multiply the rounded-up by the rounded-down number, then add the square of the distance you went up/down.

**Example:** 13² → Go down 3 to 10, up 3 to 16. 10 × 16 = 160. Add 3² = 9. Answer: 160 + 9 = **169**

---

### 6. Squaring Using the Up-and-Down Method to the Nearest Round Number

Always round to the nearest multiple of 10 for the easiest calculation. If the number ends in 6–9, round up; if 1–4, round down; if 5, go both ways.

**Example:** 77² → Round up 3 to 80, down 3 to 74. 80 × 74 = 5920. Add 3² = 9. Answer: **5929**

**Example:** 96² → Round up 4 to 100, down 4 to 92. 100 × 92 = 9200. Add 4² = 16. Answer: **9216**

---

### 7. Basic Algebra Behind Multiplication and Squaring Tricks

The distributive law explains the split-number method: (b + c) × a = (b × a) + (c × a). The squaring trick uses: A² = (A + d)(A − d) + d², where d is the distance to the nearest round number.

**Example:** 42 × 7 = (40 + 2) × 7 = (40 × 7) + (2 × 7) = 280 + 14 = **294**

**Example:** 41² = (40)(42) + 1² = 1680 + 1 = **1681** (using A² = (A−d)(A+d) + d² with d=1)

---

## Chapter 3: Intermediate Multiplication

### 1. Multiplying 2-Digit × 2-Digit Numbers

Break one of the numbers into its component parts and perform two 2-by-1 multiplications, then add the results.

**Example:** 46 × 42 → (40 × 46) + (2 × 46) = 1840 + 92 = **1932**

---

### 2. The Addition Method for Two-Digit Multiplication

Break up the second number and add the partial products. Choose the number with the smaller last digit to minimize the addition.

**Example:** 48 × 73 → (70 × 48) + (3 × 48) = 3360 + 144 = **3504**

---

### 3. Shortcut for Multiplying by 11

To multiply a two-digit number by 11, add the two digits and insert the sum between them. If the sum exceeds 9, carry.

**Example:** 54 × 11 → 5 + 4 = 9 → insert between 5 and 4 → **594**

**Example:** 85 × 11 → 8 + 5 = 13 → place 3, carry 1 → **935**

---

### 4. The Subtraction Method for Two-Digit Multiplication

Round one number up to the nearest multiple of 10, multiply, then subtract the excess.

**Example:** 59 × 17 → (60 × 17) − (1 × 17) = 1020 − 17 = **1003**

**Example:** 89 × 72 → (90 × 72) − (1 × 72) = 6480 − 72 = **6408**

---

### 5. The Factoring Method for Two-Digit Multiplication

If one number can be factored into single-digit factors, convert the 2×2 problem into sequential 2×1 and 3×1 problems.

**Example:** 75 × 63 = 75 × 9 × 7 = 675 × 7 = **4725**

**Example:** 57 × 24 = 57 × 8 × 3 = 456 × 3 = **1368**

---

### 6. Friendly Products

When using the factoring method, look for multiplications that produce a zero in the middle digit, making subsequent addition easier.

**Example:** 67 × 42 = 67 × 6 × 7 = 402 × 7 = **2814** (402 has a 0 in the middle, making the 3×1 easier)

**Example:** 43 × 56 = 43 × 7 × 8 = 301 × 8 = **2408**

---

### 7. Creatively Choosing a Multiplication Method

For any 2×2 problem, you can use addition, subtraction, or factoring. Choose whichever produces the simplest calculations.

**Example:** 73 × 49 — By subtraction: (50 × 73) − (1 × 73) = 3650 − 73 = **3577**. By factoring: 73 × 7 × 7 = 511 × 7 = **3577**. The factoring method is easiest here because 511 has a convenient form.

---

### 8. Squaring Three-Digit Numbers

Round up or down to the nearest multiple of 100. Multiply the two resulting numbers (a 3×1 problem), then add the square of the distance.

**Example:** 193² → Round up 7 to 200, down 7 to 186. 200 × 186 = 37,200. Add 7² = 49. Answer: **37,249**

**Example:** 706² → Round down 6 to 700, up 6 to 712. 712 × 700 = 498,400. Add 6² = 36. Answer: **498,436**

---

### 9. Cubing Two-Digit Numbers

Use the formula: A³ = (A − d) × A × (A + d) + d² × A, where d is the distance to the nearest multiple of 10.

**Example:** 13³ → d = 3. (10 × 13 × 16) + (9 × 13). 13 × 16 = 208, so 10 × 208 = 2080. 9 × 13 = 117. Total: 2080 + 117 = **2197**

**Example:** 35³ → d = 5. (30 × 35 × 40) + (25 × 35). 30 × 1400 = 42,000. 25 × 35 = 875. Total: 42,000 + 875 = **42,875**

## Chapter 4: Mental Division

### 1. Division by a One-Digit Divisor

Divide left to right, determining one digit of the answer at a time, simplifying the problem progressively.

**Example:** 179 ÷ 7 → 7 × 20 = 140 (answer starts with 20). 179 − 140 = 39. 39 ÷ 7 = 5 remainder 4. Answer: **25 remainder 4**

---

### 2. Determining the Number of Digits in the Answer Before Dividing

Compare the dividend to powers of the divisor times 10, 100, etc. to determine how many digits the quotient will have.

**Example:** 675 ÷ 8 → Since 675 is between 8 × 10 = 80 and 8 × 100 = 800, the answer is a two-digit number (80-something). 8 × 80 = 640, so the answer starts with 8. 675 − 640 = 35. 35 ÷ 8 = 4 remainder 3. Answer: **84 remainder 3**

---

### 3. Technique for Storing the Answer Using the "Rule of Thumb"

Use fingers to hold intermediate digits. For digits 6–9, place your thumb on: pinky (6), ring finger (7), middle finger (8), index finger (9). Hold the hundreds digit on the left hand, tens digit on the right.

**Example:** For 4579 ÷ 6 = 763 remainder 1. Hold 7 on left hand (thumb on ring finger), 6 on right hand (thumb on pinky), then say the computed ones digit (3) and remainder (1).

---

### 4. Division by a Two-Digit Divisor

Determine the tens digit of the quotient first by estimating, then subtract to find the new remainder and determine the ones digit.

**Example:** 597 ÷ 14 → 14 × 40 = 560 (so the answer is 40-something). 597 − 560 = 37. 14 × 2 = 28. Answer: 42 remainder 9, or **42 9/14**

---

### 5. Decimalization: Converting Fractions into Decimals

Memorize common fraction-to-decimal conversions. Fractions with denominators 2–11 have repeating or terminating decimals that follow elegant patterns.

**Example:** 1/7 = 0.142857142857... (the sequence 142857 repeats). 2/7 = 0.285714..., 3/7 = 0.428571..., etc. — the same six digits, just starting at a different point.

**Example:** Ninths are trivially easy: 1/9 = 0.111..., 2/9 = 0.222..., up to 8/9 = 0.888...

---

### 6. Divisibility Tests

Quick rules to determine whether a number is divisible by various divisors, without doing the division.

---

### 7. Divisibility Tests for 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 11, and 17

- **By 2:** Last digit is even.
- **By 3:** Sum of digits is divisible by 3.
- **By 4:** Last two digits form a number divisible by 4.
- **By 5:** Last digit is 0 or 5.
- **By 6:** Divisible by both 2 and 3.
- **By 7:** Add or subtract a multiple of 7 to make the number end in 0, drop the 0, and repeat. **Example:** 5292 → subtract 42 = 5250 → drop 0 = 525 → add 35 = 560 → drop 0 = 56 = 7 × 8. ✓
- **By 8:** Last three digits form a number divisible by 8.
- **By 9:** Sum of digits is divisible by 9.
- **By 11:** Alternately subtract and add digits from right to left; result is 0 or a multiple of 11. **Example:** 8492 → 8 − 4 + 9 − 2 = 11. ✓
- **By 17:** Similar to the method for 7 — add/subtract multiples of 17 to create trailing zeros.

---

### 8. Fraction Operations

Fractions are small division problems. They can be simplified, added, subtracted, multiplied, and divided using standard rules.

---

### 9. Multiplying Fractions

Multiply numerators together and denominators together.

**Example:** 2/3 × 4/5 = (2×4)/(3×5) = **8/15**

---

### 10. Dividing Fractions

Flip the second fraction (take the reciprocal) and multiply.

**Example:** 2/3 ÷ 4/5 = 2/3 × 5/4 = **10/12** = 5/6

---

### 11. Simplifying Fractions

Divide the numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor.

**Example:** 25/35 → Divide both by 5 → **5/7**

---

### 12. Adding Fractions with the Same Denominator

Add the numerators and keep the same denominator.

**Example:** 1/5 + 3/5 = **4/5**

---

### 13. Adding Fractions with Different Denominators

Find a common denominator, convert each fraction, then add.

**Example:** 1/3 + 2/5 → Convert to fifteenths: 5/15 + 6/15 = **11/15**

---

### 14. Subtracting Fractions

Same as adding, but subtract the numerators.

**Example:** 3/5 − 1/5 = **2/5**

**Example:** 1/2 − 7/8 = 4/8 − 7/8 = **−3/8**

## Chapter 5: Guesstimation

### 1. Estimating Sums

Round numbers to the nearest hundred or thousand and add.

**Example:** 8,367 + 5,819 ≈ 8,400 + 5,800 = **14,200** (exact: 14,186, error < 0.1%)

---

### 2. Estimating Differences

Round to the second or third significant digit and subtract.

**Example:** 8,367 − 5,819 ≈ 8,400 − 5,800 = **2,600** (exact: 2,548)

---

### 3. Estimating Quotients

Determine the magnitude of the answer first, then divide the leading digits.

**Example:** 57,867 ÷ 6 ≈ 58,000 ÷ 6. Since 6 × 9,000 = 54,000, the answer is about 9,000. More precisely: 58 − 54 = 4, so 4/6 ≈ 0.667, giving ~**9,667** (exact: 9,645).

---

### 4. Estimating Products

Round one number up and the other down by the same amount for best accuracy.

**Example:** 88 × 54 ≈ 90 × 52 = **4,680** (exact: 4,752; error ~1.5%)

---

### 5. Estimating Square Roots

Find the nearest perfect square, divide the original number by its root, then average the root and quotient.

**Example:** √19 → 4² = 16 is closest. 19 ÷ 4 = 4.75. Average: (4 + 4.75)/2 = **4.375** (exact: 4.359)

---

### 6. The Divide-and-Average Method for Square Roots

Guess a root, divide the number by your guess, then average the guess with the result for a better estimate.

**Example:** √87 → Guess 9 (since 9² = 81). 87 ÷ 9 = 9.66. Average: (9 + 9.66)/2 = **9.33** (exact: 9.327)

---

### 7. Calculating Tips Quickly

For 15%: find 10%, halve it for 5%, and add. For 20%: double 10%. For 25%: divide by 4 (halve twice).

**Example:** 15% of $67: 10% = $6.70, 5% = $3.35, total = **$10.05**

---

### 8. Calculating Taxes Quickly

Compute the whole-percent tax, then adjust for fractional percentages by dividing and adding.

**Example:** 6.5% on $58: 6% of 58 = $3.48, then add 0.5% (half of $58 = 29 cents): $3.48 + $0.29 = **$3.77**

---

### 9. Calculating Interest Quickly

Rule of 70: divide 70 by the interest rate to find how many years it takes to double your money. Rule of 110: divide 110 by the rate to find the tripling time.

**Example:** At 5% interest, money doubles in 70 ÷ 5 = **14 years**. At 7%, it triples in 110 ÷ 7 ≈ **15.7 years**.

---

### 10. Applying Estimation to Everyday Math

Round grocery prices to the nearest 50¢ for running totals; use order-of-magnitude estimates for large calculations.

**Example:** Items costing $1.39, $0.87, $2.46, $0.61, $3.29, $2.99 round to $1.50, $1.00, $2.50, $0.50, $3.50, $3.00 = **$12.00** (exact total ≈ $11.61)

---

## Chapter 6: Pencil-and-Paper Math

### 1. Adding Columns of Numbers

Add digits in each column from top to bottom, carrying as needed. Check by adding again from bottom to top.

**Example:** 4328 + 884 + 620 + 1477 + 617 + 725 = **8651** (add rightmost column: 8+4+0+7+7+5 = 31, write 1 carry 3, etc.)

---

### 2. Mod Sums for Checking Addition

Sum the digits of each number until you get a single digit (the mod sum). The mod sum of the total should equal the mod sum of the individual mod sums added together.

**Example:** Mod sum of 4328: 4+3+2+8 = 17 → 1+7 = 8. Do this for all numbers, add their mod sums, and reduce. Compare with the mod sum of the answer. If they match, the answer is likely correct.

---

### 3. Fast Subtraction on Paper

Subtract right to left on paper, then check by adding the answer to the subtracted number to verify you get the original number.

**Example:** 65,717 − 38,491 = **27,226**. Check: 27,226 + 38,491 = 65,717 ✓

---

### 4. Square Roots with Pencil and Paper

Use a doubling-and-dividing procedure similar to long division. Pair digits from the decimal point, find the largest square below each segment, and double/divide to find successive digits.

**Example:** √19 = 4.358... → 4² = 16 ≤ 19. Subtract: 19 − 16 = 3. Bring down 00 → 300. Double 4 = 8_. 83 × 3 = 249 ≤ 300. Next digit is 3. Continue for more precision.

---

### 5. Pencil-and-Paper Multiplication

Standard pencil-and-paper multiplication can be sped up using the criss-cross method (below).

---

### 6. Criss-Cross Multiplication

Write the answer on one line, working right to left. For a 2×2 problem: (1) multiply the ones digits; (2) criss-cross (ones×tens + tens×ones); (3) multiply the tens digits, adding any carry.

**Example:** 47 × 34 → Step 1: 7×4 = 28, write 8 carry 2. Step 2: 2 + (4×4) + (3×7) = 2 + 16 + 21 = 39, write 9 carry 3. Step 3: 3 + (3×4) = 15. Answer: **1598**

---

### 7. Casting Out Elevens to Check Results

Alternately subtract and add digits from right to left. If negative, add 11. Compare the results for the original numbers and the answer.

**Example:** 853 × 762 = 649,986. Check: 853 → 3−5+8 = 6; 762 → 2−6+7 = 3. Product of checks: 6 × 3 = 18 → 8−1 = 7. Answer check: 649,986 → 6−8+9−9+4−6 = −4, add 11 = 7. ✓

---

## Chapter 7: Memorizing Numbers

### 1. Mnemonics for Memorizing Numbers

Convert numbers to consonant sounds, add vowels to form words, then link words into memorable stories.

**Example:** To remember 129736279332826l, convert pairs to words: 12→"tiny," 97→"book," 362→"machine," 793→"kaboom," 32→"moon," 8261→"finished." Story: "I found a *tiny book* inside a *machine* that went *kaboom* and tossed me to the *moon* where I was *finished*."

---

### 2. The Phonetic Code: Converting Digits into Consonant Sounds

Each digit 0–9 maps to specific consonant sounds: 1=t/d, 2=n, 3=m, 4=r, 5=l, 6=j/ch/sh, 7=k/g, 8=f/v, 9=p/b, 0=s/z. Vowels and the sounds h, w, y can be added freely.

**Example:** 32 = m-n → "moon," "man," "money." 86 = f-sh → "fish." 47 = r-k → "rock."

---

### 3. Converting Numbers into Words

Place vowels between or around the consonant sounds to form real or memorable words.

**Example:** 42 = r-n → "rain," "rhino," "Reno." 93 = p/b-m → "bomb," "palm," "beam."

---

### 4. Memorizing Long Sequences of Numbers

Break the sequence into two- or three-digit chunks, convert each to a word, and create a story linking the words.

**Example:** First 24 digits of π (3.14159265358979323846264): "My turtle Pancho will, my love, pick up my new mover, Ginger."

---

### 5. Memorizing Dates

Convert the year, month, or historical date into words using the phonetic code.

**Example:** 1776 → 1-7-7-6 = t-k-k-sh → "attack ash" or "tick cash."

---

### 6. Memorizing Phone Numbers

Break a seven- or ten-digit phone number into chunks and convert each to a word.

**Example:** 867-5309 → 867 = "fish hook" or "fog," 5309 = "lemonsoup."

---

### 7. Using Mnemonics to Support Large Mental Calculations

During complex calculations, convert intermediate results to mnemonic words to free up working memory.

**Example:** When squaring 342: 300 × 384 = 115,200. Store "115" as the word "title" and hold "200" on two fingers. Compute 42² = 1764, then add to "title 2" (115,200) → **116,964**.

---

### 8. Memory Magic

Use the phonetic code to memorize 16 or more random digits called out by an audience, by converting them to words linked by a silly story.

**Example:** Digits 1,2,9,7,3,6,2,7,9,3,3,2,8,2,6,1 → 12="tiny," 97="book," 362="machine," 793="kaboom," 32="moon," 8261="finished." Story as above.

## Chapter 8: Advanced Multiplication

### 1. Squaring Four-Digit Numbers

Round to the nearest multiple of 1000, multiply to get the first part, then add the square of the distance (a three-digit square). Use mnemonics to store intermediate results.

**Example:** 4267² → Round down 267 to 4000, up 267 to 4534. 4534 × 4000 = 18,136,000. Store "136" as "damage." Compute 267² = 71,289. Add: 18,136,000 + 71,289 = **18,207,289**

---

### 2. Multiplying 3-Digit × 2-Digit Numbers

Use the factoring method if possible, or the addition/subtraction method.

**Example:** 846 × 37. By criss-cross or by: 37 × 846 = 37 × 800 + 37 × 46 = 29,600 + 1,702 = **31,302**

---

### 3. The Factoring Method for 3-by-2 Multiplication

Factor the two-digit number and perform sequential multiplications.

**Example:** 225 × 54 = 225 × 9 × 6 = 2025 × 6 = **12,150**

---

### 4. The Addition Method for 3-by-2 Multiplication

Break the two-digit number into tens and ones, perform two separate multiplications, and add.

**Example:** 853 × 47 → (40 × 853) + (7 × 853) = 34,120 + 5,971 = **40,091**

---

### 5. The Subtraction Method for 3-by-2 Multiplication

Round the two-digit number up and subtract the excess.

**Example:** 538 × 29 → (30 × 538) − (1 × 538) = 16,140 − 538 = **15,602**

---

### 6. Squaring Five-Digit Numbers

Extend the four-digit technique: round to the nearest multiple of 10,000, compute a 5×1 product, add the square of the distance (a four-digit square using the same technique recursively).

**Example:** 46,792² → Round to 47,000 and 46,584. Compute 47,000 × 46,584 and add 208². The process is analogous but longer.

---

### 7. Multiplying 3-Digit × 3-Digit Numbers

Use one of several methods: factoring, close-together, addition, subtraction, or the when-all-else-fails method.

**Example:** 107 × 111 → Close-together method (base 100): 100 × 118 + 7 × 11 = 11,800 + 77 = **11,877**

---

### 8. The Factoring Method for 3-by-3 Multiplication

Factor one number and perform sequential multiplications.

**Example:** 656 × 427 = 656 × 61 × 7 = 656 × 61 = 40,016; 40,016 × 7 = **280,112**

---

### 9. The Close-Together Method

When two numbers are close to a convenient base z: (z+a)(z+b) = z(z+a+b) + ab.

**Example:** 396 × 387 (both near 400): 400 × 383 = 153,200. Add (−4)(−13) = 52. Answer: **153,252**

**Example:** 109 × 104 (both near 100): 100 × 113 = 11,300. Add 9 × 4 = 36. Answer: **11,336**

---

### 10. The Addition Method for 3-by-3 Multiplication

Break one number into a convenient two-digit part plus a one-digit part.

**Example:** 732 × 308 → (300 × 732) + (8 × 732) = 219,600 + 5,856 = **225,456**

---

### 11. The Subtraction Method for 3-by-3 Multiplication

Round one number up to a convenient value and subtract the excess.

**Example:** 719 × 247 → (720 × 247) − (1 × 247) = 177,840 − 247 = **177,593**

---

### 12. The When-All-Else-Fails Method

Break the problem into a 3×1, a 2×1, and a 2×2, summing results as you go. Use mnemonics to store intermediate totals.

**Example:** 851 × 527 → (500 × 851) = 425,500. Then (27 × 800) = 21,600; subtotal 447,100. Then (27 × 51) = 1,377; grand total = **448,477**

---

### 13. Multiplying 5-Digit × 5-Digit Numbers

Break each number into a two-digit "thousands" part and a three-digit "ones" part. Compute four sub-problems: a 2×2, two 3×2s, and a 3×3, summing with carries.

**Example:** 27,639 × 52,196 → Compute 52 × 639 = 33,228, 27 × 196 = 5,292, sum = 38,520 (thousands). Then 52 × 27 = 1,404 (millions). Then 639 × 196 = 125,244. Grand total: **1,442,645,244**

---

### 14. Using the Phonetic Code to Store Intermediate Results in Large Multiplications

During large problems, convert intermediate sums to mnemonic words.

**Example:** While computing 27,639 × 52,196: store 33,228 as "Mom, no knife," then after adding 5,292, store 38,520 as "Movie lines." This frees mental space for the remaining calculations.

## Chapter 9: Mathematical Magic

### 1. Psychic Math

Have someone think of a number, double it, add 12, divide by 2, and subtract the original number. The answer is always 6 (half of whatever was added in step 2).

**Example:** Secret number = 17. Double: 34. Add 12: 46. Divide by 2: 23. Subtract 17: **6**.

---

### 2. The Magic 1089

Have someone write a three-digit number with decreasing digits, reverse and subtract, then add the result to its reverse. The answer is always 1089.

**Example:** Start with 851. Reverse = 158. 851 − 158 = 693. Reverse of 693 = 396. 693 + 396 = **1089**

---

### 3. Missing-Digit Tricks

Multiply 1089 by any number (producing a multiple of 9). Have someone read all but one digit — the missing digit is whatever makes the digit sum reach the next multiple of 9.

**Example:** 1089 × 256 = 278,784. If someone reads 2, 4, 7, 8, 8 (sum = 29), the missing digit is 36 − 29 = **7**.

---

### 4. Leapfrog Addition

Have a volunteer write two numbers, then create a Fibonacci-like sequence of 10 numbers. The sum equals 11 times the 7th number. The ratio of the 10th to the 9th number is always approximately 1.618 (the golden ratio).

**Example:** Start with 9, 2 → sequence: 9, 2, 11, 13, 24, 37, 61, 98, 159, 257. Sum = 11 × 61 = **671**. Ratio: 257/159 ≈ **1.616**

---

### 5. Magic Squares

A magic square is a grid where every row, column, and diagonal sums to the same number.

**Example:** A 4×4 magic square using numbers 1–16, where every row, column, and diagonal sums to 34:

| 8 | 11 | 14 | 1 |
|---|---|---|---|
| 13 | 2 | 7 | 12 |
| 3 | 16 | 9 | 6 |
| 10 | 5 | 4 | 15 |

---

### 6. How to Construct a Magic Square

To make a magic square summing to any number N: subtract 34 from N, divide by 4 (getting quotient Q and remainder R). Add Q to most cells of the base 34-square, and add (Q + R) to cells containing 13, 14, 15, or 16.

**Example:** For N = 67: 67 − 34 = 33, 33 ÷ 4 = 8 remainder 1. Add 8 to most cells, add 9 to cells with 13–16. Every row/column/diagonal now sums to **67**.

---

### 7. Quick Cube Roots

To find the cube root of a perfect cube: (1) look at the thousands digits to determine the tens digit of the root; (2) look at the last digit to determine the units digit (since each digit 0–9 has a unique cube ending).

**Example:** Cube root of 314,432 → 314 is between 6³ = 216 and 7³ = 343, so the tens digit is 6. The cube ends in 2, and 8³ = 512 ends in 2, so the units digit is 8. Cube root = **68**.

---

### 8. Simplified Square Roots

For perfect squares of two-digit numbers: (1) determine the tens digit from the hundreds portion; (2) narrow the units digit to two candidates, then compare against the square of the midpoint to pick the right one.

**Example:** √7569 → 75 is between 8² = 64 and 9² = 81 → tens digit is 8. Last digit 9 → could be 83 or 87. Since 85² = 7225 and 7569 > 7225, the answer is the larger: **87**.

---

### 9. The "Amazing" Sum

Using pre-constructed card sets whose columns always sum to the same totals, a performer can predict the sum of four three-digit numbers selected randomly.

**Example:** Three sets of cards are constructed so that the A-column digits always sum to 20, B-column to 23, and C-column to 17. No matter which cards are selected, the four three-digit numbers always sum to **2247**.

---

### 10. Determining the Day of the Week for Any Date

Add three codes: Month Code + Date + Year Code. Subtract the largest multiple of 7. The result gives the day (1=Mon through 7=Sun).

**Example:** December 3, 2006 → Month code 4 + Date 3 + Year code 0 = 7 = **Sunday**

---

### 11. Month Codes, Year Codes, and Leap-Year Rules

Month codes: Jan=6, Feb=2, Mar=2, Apr=5, May=0, Jun=3, Jul=5, Aug=1, Sep=4, Oct=6, Nov=2, Dec=4. (In leap years, Jan=5, Feb=1.) Year codes for 2000+x: compute x + (x÷4, ignoring remainder), then reduce mod 7.

**Example:** Year code for 2061: 61 + 15 = 76, then 76 − 70 = **6**.

---

### 12. Date Calculations in the Gregorian Calendar

For dates in the 1900s, add 1 to the year code calculated as if it were a 2000s date. For the 1800s, add 3. For the 2000s, add 0. This allows calculation for any date in the Gregorian calendar.

**Example:** July 4, 1776 → Calculate the year code for "76" as if in the 2000s: 76 + 19 = 95, mod 7 = 4. Add century adjustment for 1700s (which is 5): 4 + 5 = 9, mod 7 = 2. Month code for July = 5. Total: 5 + 4 + 2 = 11, mod 7 = 4 = **Thursday**.
