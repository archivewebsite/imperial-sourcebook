This is the **Unit Circle** — a fundamental diagram in trigonometry often called the "One Circle to Rule Them All" because nearly every trigonometric concept flows from it.

## What It Shows

The circle has **radius 1** and is centered at the origin. Each point on the circumference represents an angle measured from the positive x-axis:

- **Counter-clockwise** = positive angles
- **Clockwise** = negative angles (not shown here, but implied)

## The Two Labels at Each Point

For any angle θ, the coordinates are **(cos θ, sin θ)**:

| Angle (degrees) | Angle (radians) | Coordinates (cos θ, sin θ) |
|---|---|---|
| 0° | 0 | (1, 0) |
| 30° | π/6 | (√3/2, 1/2) |
| 45° | π/4 | (√2/2, √2/2) |
| 60° | π/3 | (1/2, √3/2) |
| 90° | π/2 | (0, 1) |

This pattern continues around all four quadrants, with signs flipping according to the quadrant rules (ASTC: All, Sine, Tangent, Cosine).

## Why It "Rules Them All"

1. **Sine & Cosine**: The x-coordinate is cos θ; the y-coordinate is sin θ.
2. **Tangent**: tan θ = sin θ / cos θ = y/x (rise over run).
3. **All other trig functions** (secant, cosecant, cotangent) are just reciprocals.
4. **Reference angles**: Any angle can be reduced to an equivalent angle between 0 and 2π.
5. **The Pythagorean identity**: Since x² + y² = 1 on this circle, we get cos²θ + sin²θ = 1.

## How to Read the Pattern

Notice the three coordinate values that appear everywhere: **0, 1/2, √2/2, √3/2, and 1**. They simply shift between x and y depending on the angle:

- At 30° (π/6): x is the larger value (√3/2), y is the smaller (1/2)
- At 60° (π/3): they swap — x is 1/2, y is √3/2
- At 45° (π/4): they're equal (√2/2, √2/2)

In quadrants II, III, and IV, the **absolute values** stay the same; only the **signs** change based on whether x or y is negative in that quadrant.

Master this one circle, and you've mastered the foundation of all trigonometry.
