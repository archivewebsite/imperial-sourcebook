## And then there’s Trump… (read until the end)

![American traveler walking through a historic European cobblestone street toward a Medieval type church.](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:2000/format:webp/0*tiEtlOaiumoRYMoY)

Most Americans grew up with a simple idea: having a U.S. passport meant you were welcomed everywhere.

Those days have gone the way of the American dream.

In 2026, traveling to, and especially moving to Europe is becoming more complicated. Not because countries suddenly don’t want Americans, but because local pressures are forcing governments to change how they handle tourism, housing, and immigration.

This isn’t about one country. It’s happening across the continent.

And the reasons are surprisingly similar.

### It Starts With Housing

Take Portugal.

Portugal with its Golden Visa was the golden ticket out of America for people who could afford it.

==The Golden Visa== ==is a residency-by-investment program that allows non-EU citizens to get a Portuguese residence permit in exchange for a significant financial contribution to the country.==

It’s famous because it only requires you to spend about seven days a year in Portugal to keep your residency and eventually apply for citizenship after five years.

Add to that the warm weather and the lower costs (for the Americans earning in dollars, not for the locals), and you’ve created one of the easiest places for rich foreigners to settle.

And settle they did. Until it was too much for the locals to take.

Housing prices in cities like Lisbon went up fast. Local wages didn’t. That gap created tension and locals started feeling pushed out of their own neighborhoods.

The government responded by removing some of the easiest residency options, like proposing to double the five-year wait for citizenship, permanently removing real estate investment as a qualifying route, and introducing mandatory civic and cultural tests to ensure foreigners are more deeply integrated before they can naturalize.

So the vibe of the Golden Visa has shifted from “rich people buying villas” to “investors navigating a digital bureaucracy.”

Portugal is still open. But it’s no longer the simple, low-cost move it once was.

Spain is facing a similar situation.

In cities like Barcelona or Valencia, protests have broken out over rising rents and overcrowding. Residents blame short-term rentals and wealthy newcomers for driving up costs.

I visited Barcelona this January. It was cold, it was raining, it was windy, and still, the streets were flooded with tourists. Every day. In umbrella-flipping wind and cold rain, the city was packed.

I still loved it. Rain or shine, it’s still Barcelona. But that’s just the problem. Tourists loving it so much has made it impossible for the locals to live there.

For the locals, the frustration has become visible. Tourists have been openly told to leave certain neighborhoods. They were sprayed with water. They were chased out of places. And I get it. It feels like a massive invasion that doesn’t only crowd the streets but drives locals out of the place they grew up in because they can no longer afford rent.

Again, Spain isn’t closed. But the mood has shifted.

### Making It More Expensive to Stay

No country is actually keeping Americans out — they’re just making it harder financially.

The Netherlands is a good example.

Amsterdam’s reputation as an expat-friendly hub is currently under strain due to a perfect storm of legislative changes and the peak of a decade-long housing crisis.

At the same time, the 2026 housing market has hit a breaking point; new rent regulations have ==ironically== caused landlords to pull properties off the market, leaving international workers to navigate record-high prices and a historic shortage of supply.

==Instead of saying “don’t come,” the system now says: “be prepared to pay more”. Much more==.

Greece has taken a similar path, but more directly.

The country raised the minimum investment required for residency in popular areas, like Athens and the islands. The minimum investment for a Golden Visa has jumped from €250,000 to €800,000 — that’s more than triple the price of entry.

==The message is clear: if you want to move to Europe, it won’t be cheap anymore.==

### Too Many Visitors, Not Enough Space

Europe is small. And crowded. Small houses, narrow streets, ==tiny weird-looking cars (the French ones, obviously).==

But the problem isn’t just land mass, it’s population density.

The EU is about half the size of the US, but it’s packed with 100 million more people.

In places like Italy, the issue isn’t just housing — it’s volume. Venice is overwhelmed with visitors, because who wouldn’t want to visit a medieval city perched on massive pillars above water, where you go from one place to another with gondolas Sounds romantic, doesn’t it

Well, not so much for the people living there, but definitely for the Italian economy. So the response was easy: introduce fees just to enter for the day.

It’s like an expensive restaurant selecting its clientele through pricing alone. It doesn’t stop tourism completely, but it’s slowing it down, bringing more money into the economy at the same time. More money, less effort, less angry locals. Win-win-win.

Iceland faces a different version of the same problem.

Its population is small, but the number of visitors has exploded. That puts pressure on roads, nature, and ==local services==.

So the country is adding environmental taxes and stricter rules.

They brought back a bed tax for all hotel stays and introduced a new daily infrastructure fee for cruise ship passengers to fund nature conservation. To keep housing affordable, the government has also banned companies from using residential apartments as Airbnbs and limited individual hosts to only 90 rental days per year.

==The goal isn’t to push people away. It’s to protect what’s already there.==

### Tighter Rules and More Paperwork

Some countries are tightening systems that were already complex. To put it lightly. In truer terms, Europe is a bureaucratic hell.

For Americans used to the simpler and more straightforward American system, it’ll be a paperwork hell reality of Kafkaesque proportions. Kafka lived in Prague — that should tell you something.

Spain and France for example are well known for their bureaucracy, and that hasn’t changed. If anything, it’s becoming more demanding for people who want to stay long-term.

There’s more paperwork, more requirements, and less flexibility.

The United Kingdom is also becoming more selective.

Visa requirements are stricter. Salary thresholds are higher. Even healthcare fees for foreign workers have increased significantly.

This doesn’t mean Americans can’t move there. But it does mean fewer people qualify. Or want to, when they see what it really means.

Sure, you might be thinking that this bureaucracy doesn’t only apply to Americans wanting to move in, but to everybody living there, and you wouldn’t be wrong. But those people are used to it.

They’ve lived their whole lives bringing yet another notarized document to god knows what slow and moronic government institution.

It is annoying for everybody, of course. But it’s deeply disturbing for the nation that has such a deep disgust for inefficiency that they invented the drive-through.

These people (the Americans, obviously) won’t even wait for a hot meal. Europe expects them to wait for some stupid government employee that should have given them the damn document three months ago I don’t think so.

### A Different Kind of Mindset

There’s also a cultural shift happening.

In many places, there’s a growing expectation that visitors behave more like guests — and less like temporary residents reshaping the local economy.

That means: respecting local prices and systems, understanding housing pressure, and adapting to rules instead of working around them.

This isn’t new. But it’s becoming more visible. ==The U.S. is built on individuality, strong personalities, and not being afraid to ask for what you want. Loudly.==

The cultural clash is huge.

### And then there’s Trump

==I don’t need to tell you just how much Europe hates Trump, MAGA, and every red hat in sight.==

And although everybody understands not all Americans voted for Trump and we’re all in the same boat because of this red plague that has been unleashed upon the world, at some point it gets difficult to welcome people who one way or another remind them of one of history’s most damaging leaders.

It’s not even a choice, it’s a knee-jerk reaction.

And wherever they go, ==Americans are obviously… well, obviously American. They’re loud, they’re always wearing T-shirts and sneakers, and they put ketchup on pizza==.

Nothing wrong with that, it’s just that when Trump and his orcs roam the earth, it’s best if you’re not associated with them in any way.

That’s why other English-speaking tourists are now making it a point to show the countries they visit that they’re not Americans. ==Canadians sew the maple leaf on their backpacks==, Aussies speak English with a strong Australian accent, call everybody ‘mate’, the Brits keep the stiffest upper lip, or just openly say it: ‘I’m not American!’

==Of course this is an exaggeration for the sake of entertainment.==

But any joke has a grain of truth to it, and that truth is… ==it’s the worst time to be an American. Who would have thought==
