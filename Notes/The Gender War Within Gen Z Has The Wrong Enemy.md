Let’s start something

## We all got trapped inside the same zero-sum cage

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:2000/format:webp/1*MkV9fbypCl-uX-tIokcX0w.png)

Last year at a dinner in Stockholm, eight of us were crowded around a table that was too small, the way the best dinner tables always are. Lars had made a lamb stew that had been cooking since noon and the whole apartment smelled of rosemary and warmth.

We were talking about work. Lately, work is what we default to, the way older generations talked about the weather or sports. One person brought up a promotion that never came. Another described the kind of job that looks impressive from the outside and hollows you out from within. I said something about trying to build on ground that won’t stop shifting, and everyone gave that tired laugh that means: *yes, exactly.*

Then the conversation turned to dating, as it always does at tables like this one, where everyone knows each other’s monsters in the closet.

*“The apps are just broken,”* said Sara, who is 30 and has been on every platform and deleted them all. *“Like structurally broken. You match, you text for a week, and then nothing. You’re basically pen pals with people you’ll never meet.”*

*“I mean, at least you get matches,”* said Mikael, and everyone laughed, and he laughed too, but there was something flat underneath it.

*“Oh come on,”* said Sara. *“Look at you. You definitely do fine.”*

*“I really don’t,”* he said, still smiling. *“And honestly, I’m done trying to figure out if it’s me or the whole situation.”* He tore off a piece of bread. *“Because it feels like the whole situation is broken. Like, the ground rules are messed up.”*

*“You mean the apps”* someone asked.

*“Not just the apps,”* he said, searching for it. *“The whole setup. The expectations. Who we’re supposed to be right now. What we’re supposed to bring to the table. I can’t tell if I’m coming up short… or if the feminist bar itself is just completely unhinged.”*

Nobody spoke for a moment. Someone refilled the glasses.

*“I feel that,”* Sara said, almost like talking to herself. *“Just from the other direction.”*

Mikael looked at her. *“Yeah,”* he said. *“I think we’re both feeling the same pressure, just from opposite sides of it.”*

He did not explain, and she did not press. The conversation moved on, but I carried that line with me for the rest of the night, turning it over and over.

The same pressure, just from opposite directions.

### What actually happened to us

My grandfather died of prostate cancer. He worked until his last days. His death was hard but it was legible: he had the house, the family, the job that became a story, the community that gathered at the bedside, the physical evidence of a life that had added up to something you could touch and feel. The script worked for him. Like a frozen lake in the winter, he followed it, and it held.

I am 32. I do not own property. I am not even considering being a mom. I have friends I have not seen in a year because we all live in different cities and the logistics of seeing each other require a level of planning and expense that our parents would have found baffling. I have a career I am proud of and a relationship I cherish and a life that, measured honestly, contains more than many people’s and less than the life I was told to expect. And beneath all of it, on the days when I let myself feel it, there is a low-frequency terror that has nothing to do with personal failure and everything to do with structural fact.

. Healthcare became a financial threat. Retirement became the thing people joke about before changing the subject. The food system made processed, corporate-optimized products the affordable option and then blamed individuals when their bodies broke down. Early-onset cancers are rising. Chronic illness is everywhere.

There’s a loneliness supply chain of intimacy and extortion just a click away, supported by a rent that eats half your income, meaningful work consumes the rest and the third spaces where people used to simply be together without spending money have mostly closed.

In my twenties, I started saying no to plans. No big explanation. Just a polite “can’t make it” that soon became my default.

What I did not say out loud was that, before every social event, I was doing a math problem: can I show up as I am, without pretending I am doing better than I am For a while, the answer was usually no. It is a kind of poverty that does not show up in charts. It shows up in the bathroom mirror, practicing a version of “so what have you been up to lately” that will not give you away. A version where the real answer — less than I expected, less than I hoped, less than I can admit — stays hidden.

Mikael, I think, was describing exactly this. Not a gender grievance. The sensation of a script that was supposed to work and didn’t, with nowhere obvious to put the failure.

### The zero-sum cage

A 30-country study found that 60% of Gen Z men (those born between 1997 and 2012, following Millennials and preceding Gen Alpha) say men are being asked to do too much to support equality. My first instinct was to look for the flaw in the methodology. 60% is not a fringe — it is the majority position among young men across thirty countries simultaneously, in the same years when 53% of Gen Z women call themselves feminist and only 32% of Gen Z men do, the largest gender divide ever recorded in research of this scale.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/0*aH4JD6QCmkETscgo)

Source: Millennials and Gen Z less in favour of gender equality than older generations

I couldn’t find any major flaw. I mean, few research has such wide reach. But I thought about Mikael. About the specific flatness underneath his laugh.

He is not 60% of anything. He is one person at a dinner table ranting about social facades and economic dispossession, and reaching for the nearest available explanation for why his life had not matched his expectations. ==The fact that he reached toward women, toward feminism, toward the idea of a game rewritten in someone else’s favor, rather than toward housing policy or labor market transformation or the financialization of everything that once made a life legible, is not a coincidence.== It is the result of a very specific set of forces operating on him that he cannot fully see, because no one can fully see the water they swim in. And I recognized the gesture because I have made it too, differently, toward different targets, with the same underlying confusion about what happened to the life I was supposed to be living.

Here is what the research helped me understand: what he was describing is not primarily a gender grievance. ==It is zero-sum thinking, and zero-sum thinking is what happens to people when the economy they grew up in taught them, through direct personal experience, that opportunities are finite and someone else winning means you lose.== A paper found that zero-sum beliefs are strongly associated with economic immobility. It is not ignorance or bad morals, but the rational conclusion of people who watched the ladder break and are looking around for who broke it.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1100/format:webp/1*k3DaB_SSCuzg3nNi_SavHw.png)

And zero-sum thinking has no fixed political direction. The same belief that life is a competition and there is not enough to go around can fuel both feminist anger about unfairness and resentment toward women’s gains. That is one big reason Gen Z women moved left while Gen Z men moved right: they were sorted into different stories, on purpose.

The big, structural shift that coincides with the growing gender divide is technology. If you hand a teen a smartphone, you are handing them a slot machine that never closes: games, jokes, feeds, podcasts, endless distraction.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/0*UzP2-RG_XNzefCyH.jpeg)

John Burn-Murdoch 2024

TikTok, Facebook, YouTube, Twitter, and Instagram are built to keep you scrolling. The algorithm quickly learns what gets a reaction out of you, then starts serving you more of the same, especially the most sensational, angering and negative content. Over time, that creates a ‘filter bubble’: ==you mostly see stories that match what you already believe, and you end up surrounded by people who believe it too==. Inside that kind of closed loop, extreme ideas get normalized, and people stop needing to explain themselves, because nobody is pushing back. Slurs and stereotypes go unchallenged, not because they are suddenly “proven,” but because the environment stops demanding proof.

A simple example: “Who pays for dates” is trending in the Western manosphere, framed as a rigged test where men are expected to pay while being told equality means splitting the bill. On its own, it is an awkward, solvable social question. But inside an echo chamber, it gets recycled into proof that women are unfair, which can harden into resentment that has little to do with the actual cost of dinner.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/0*6xfAeWZiK5dePxrS)

Source: Millennials and Gen Z less in favour of gender equality than older generations

that teenage boys searching for content about loneliness were fed a fourfold increase in content focused on anger directed at women within five days. Five days — less than a school week, less than the time it takes to notice a new habit forming. A teenage boy who opens TikTok in a moment of ordinary adolescent confusion, feeling invisible, feeling behind, feeling the low hum of a life that does not seem to be adding up the way he was told it would, can emerge five days later with a coherent narrative about who is responsible for his suffering, complete with a community of men who will validate and amplify it.

His parents, watching him from the doorway with his phone, may think he is safe. But the machine already has him.

### I am still ashamed, but I am also done pretending

I want to say something that I find hard to say, so I will say it here, where I am trying to be honest.

I have sat in rooms where men’s pain was being discussed and felt the specific internal resistance that says: but what about ours, but we have it worse, but this attention is coming at my expense. I recognize this now as zero-sum thinking, as the voice of someone who absorbed the same lesson Mikael absorbed, just through a different feed.

I have been that machine’s product too, in my own way, in my own filter bubble, fed my own version of the zero-sum story behind liberal media documents. I know what it feels like to spend enough time inside a particular informational ecosystem that its premises start to feel like real patterns and real facts. I know the specific cognitive click of a narrative that organizes your anxiety into something with a shape and a name across the divide — someone who is also losing, who is also being sorted, who is the wrong target for a rage that should be aimed at the architecture of their shared dispossession.

I am not proud of it. But I am also done pretending it does not happen, because pretending it does not happen is part of why the conversation stays broken.

The research on what prevents gender polarization is almost painfully simple. Close-knit communities with shared offline socialization produce ideological convergence, as Alice Evans documents in her work on gendered polarization across cultures. When men and women occupy the same physical spaces, share the same cultural references, play the same games in the same rooms, the zero-sum mentality has no space to sort them into opposing camps. The erosion of those spaces is not incidental to the gender war. It is the precondition for it.

South Korea is the laboratory where this dynamic has run furthest. 80% of Korean men in their twenties believe there is serious gender discrimination against men. Korean women are simultaneously building the most organized feminist movement in Asia. Men working brutal hours, locked out of romantic access, absorbed into a community that names women as the source of their failure. Women sharing stories of abuse, building solidarity, naming a different enemy. Same country, same economic pressures, completely different realities, and no shared physical space where the two populations encounter each other as people rather than as members of opposing forces.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/0*OfYqwd4gBy1GHJTL.png)

John Burn-Murdoch 2024

I think about this when I think about my own friendships. The close ones, the ones that have lasted, are mostly with people I spent physical time with during formative years. People I walked with, cooked with, traveled with, shared apartments with in the years before everyone could afford their own. The friendships I have tried to build primarily online, through shared intellectual or leisure interests, rarely achieve the same density. Something is missing from them, some texture that only comes from being in the same room when nothing particular is happening.

That texture is what the algorithm cannot provide and what the algorithm is replacing.

So what started to loosen my internal resistance was not better arguments. It was physical proximity to men I actually knew, watching them struggle in ways my algorithm never showed me, and finally putting my guard down long enough to have the hard conversation that could complement our ‘filtered realities.’

### The same opposite sides

I do not know what I am building toward. I have a life I am genuinely grateful for and a terror underneath it that I suspect is structural rather than personal. I do not know what my equivalent of my grandfather’s legacy is, if what I am accumulating in knowledge, experiences, connections or possessions will mean something, or if my life will collapse into work, stress, and obligations, whether there will be anyone around at the end to whom my life meant anything.

Look at our lives against the state of the world today, not the one we were promised, and the ending can look bleak in very specific ways. But there’s also so much power when unity comes together, even in the bleakest of times:

![](https://x.com/rkmtimes/status/2038083855379538088)

I think this death anxiety nightmare is more common than anyone admits. And I think the gender war among millennials and Gen Z is partly what happens when two groups are carrying the same dread, but cannot find a shared language for it, while the machine that profits from their division keeps ensuring they never do.

The young man in the manosphere and the young woman in the progressive bubble are both rational responses to an irrational situation. They are both, in different ways, describing the same absence and blaming it on each other.

And that absence, many times, is the lost ability to plan a future that feels solid enough to grow old inside.

Mikael said we, men and women, might be under the same pressure, just from opposite directions. That pressure is the revoked script, the canceled social contract, the material conditions for a legible life that were available to previous generations and are not reliably available to ours. The gender war is what it looks like when that loss has no common name, and the machine makes sure it stays that way.

We are carrying the same pressure from opposite directions: the same baseline panic of a life that was supposed to add up by now and hasn’t. A system that profits from our division feeds us separate narratives about who is responsible, keeps the zero-sum mentality running hot, and collects the revenue generated by our mutual contempt. It needs us pointed at each other. It needs the war to continue. A generation that looked across the divide and said, wait, you are also losing, you are also afraid, you also don’t know what you are building toward, would be a generation with nowhere left to direct its energy except upward, toward the architecture of the thing itself.

I think if Mikael and Sara and everyone else had stayed at that table long enough — past the discomfort, past the familiar instinct to protect our separate grievances — we would have found more in common than either of our algorithms wanted us to find. That the person across the table is not the enemy but the only witness to the same bewildering pressure, coming from the same direction, wearing a different face. That what the moment actually called for was not a better argument but a willingness to stop calculating and, for once, just listen. To look at each other without the filter and ask: how can we work this out And mean it. And wait for the answer.

==The math will not save us.==

But looking into each other’s eyes might do the trick.
