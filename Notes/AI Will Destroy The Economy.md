## One way or another.

The notion that the current AI industry is in a negative-sum game isn’t new. So much money has been dumped into this technology that we have practically bet our economy on its success. So, if it doesn’t succeed, we become the newest residents of recession town. But if this bet does pay off, then millions will lose their jobs, and our economy will collapse, sending us to the same bleak place. It’s shooting ourselves in the foot whilst screaming “innovation!” However, we are starting to see the details of how these scenarios might play out. So, let me explain.

### If AI Fails

If you have read any of my work before, you know I am *slightly* sceptical of AI, so let’s start with the AI failure scenario.

We are now starting to see how AI could fail.

Quite simply, it isn’t the productivity tool it was sold as. A Harvard Business Review study found that AI didn’t reduce work, but consistently intensified it. The employees “worked at a faster pace, took on a broader scope of tasks, and extended work into more hours of the day, often without being asked to do so.” In other words, ==AI isn’t a productivity tool, but a burnout tool.== This is supported by the recent report from Oxford Economics (read more here), which found that companies “don’t appear to be replacing workers with AI on a significant scale” and instead suggest that they are actually using the AI layoff narrative to cover up their own shortcomings. Meanwhile, Forrester principal analyst JP Gownder has said that they simply don’t see any boost to productivity from AI in any dataset. Senior US economist Ronnie Walker found something similar, stating that “We still do not find a meaningful relationship between productivity and AI adoption at the economy-wide level.” The only areas he says had productivity gains were “customer support” and “software development tasks.” Note that it isn’t software development, but specifically granular tasks within the job. That doesn’t mean it equates to software development jobs increasing productivity, without decreasing quality, as there could be (and almost certainly are) bottlenecks that AI doesn’t address.

So, even though it might feel like AI makes you more productive on an individual level, that isn’t scaling to an economic-wide impact. Which is a shame because it needs to do that.

That isn’t just my opinion, but that of Nobel laureate Geoffrey Hinton, often called one of the “Godfathers of AI” for his enormous contributions to the artificial neural network technology that powers AI. In an interview with Bloomberg, he was asked whether the eye-watering investments in AI will ever pay off. Hinton replied, “I believe that it can’t,” and elaborated, “I believe that to make money, you’re going to have to replace human labour.” Other reports back this up. As I covered in a previous article, a recent report found that the AI industry will need to generate $2 trillion in annual revenue just to pay for the data centres they plan to build by 2030. This report used **very** optimistic revenue projections to estimate that the AI industry will be $800 billion short of breaking even by 2030!

To give you an idea of that scale, Google, one of the most monopolistic companies on the planet, made $400 billion in revenue in 2025. So, just to pay for the datacentres AI runs on, the industry will need to pull in more than four times that, and AI can only generate that kind of revenue by replacing huge swaths of the job market.

So, what happens if AI doesn’t replace labour

Well, the entire industry will be cannibalised by its own debt.

As the Bank of England recently pointed out, McKinsey estimates that AI data centres will require $5.2 trillion in capital expenditure by 2030, with the vast majority of that financed through debt, such as bonds, loans and private credit. Likewise, Morgan Stanley estimates that AI infrastructure capital expenditure between 2025 and 2028 will be $2.9 trillion, with at least $1.5 trillion of that financed through external debt, ==$800 billion of which through private credit.== However, after 2028, non-debt financing routes, such as equity or parent company funding, are expected to be exhausted, so the ratio of debt in AI financing is likely to explode over the coming years.

Oh, and all the AI data centres being bought with all this debt only last three years or so (read more here). That means the AI industry won’t just have to pay the insane interest and capital on these loans, but also the insane depreciation on these assets.

==So, the AI industry needs to find that $2 trillion quick-smart, otherwise they are going to start defaulting on their debts rapidly!== And guess what happens when several trillion dollars worth of loans start to default An utterly huge recession with the potential to wipe out critical parts of our financial system. This happened in 2008 when just over $1 trillion worth of mortgage debt defaulted, triggering a massive economic crash. Oh, and like in 2008, when subprime mortgages were sold as investment-grade debt, this AI debt is also being sold as investment-grade (read more here)! That will only intensify the crash.

Okay, but what if I am wrong What if all the flaws of AI are resolved in the next few years, and the industry can generate enough revenue

### What If The AI Bet Pays Off

Well, let’s assume things go to plan, and projected job losses to AI happen.

has estimated that AI will automate the equivalent of 300 million full-time jobs. Now, it is true that AI can create new jobs. The World Economic Forum Future Jobs Report 2025 found that by 2030, AI will displace 92 million jobs globally and create 170 million new jobs, resulting in a net gain of 78 million jobs. That sounds good, until you realise that these new jobs are significantly lower-paying, and many of the jobs being taken away are entry-level roles on high-paying career ladders; as such, the average wage could be decreased. Indeed, many of these new jobs are in developing countries, where remote workers can be hired for pennies on the dollar. We have seen this with the likes of Waymo, which has automated US taxi drivers, but uses poorly paid Filipino remote workers to monitor their cars. So, despite there being new jobs, the economic buying power of developed countries could be severely impacted. However, we should take the number of new jobs being created with a pinch of salt, as the World Economic Forum has historically been biased towards neoliberalism and corporate interests, so this report is likely highly optimistic.

So, based on current projections, it looks like if AI works as promised, tens of millions, if not hundreds of millions of jobs could be effectively wiped out. That is, both directly and through the economic buying power of working people in the developed world, reduced by the equivalent of tens of millions, or hundreds of millions, of job losses. Again, for some sense of scale, roughly 30 million individuals lost their jobs in the wake of the 2008 crash.

So, why is that bad Well, because workers are way more leveraged than they used to be!

Back in 2005, the average level of debt (including mortgage) of a US citizen was $51,062.02. In 2025, that number has risen to $105,056. Even taking inflation into account, that is a 27% increase. Not only that, but private credit, a debt market known as “shadow banking” because of its murkiness, is currently booming, with the market currently worth over $2 trillion. In fact, so much of this lower-regulated debt has been lent out through private credit that many bankers think it alone is another 2008 subprime mortgage crisis waiting to happen, including Lloyd Blankfein, who served at the helm of Goldman from 2006 until 2018, and Jamie Dimon, JPMorgan’s current chief.

This is a perfect storm for a catastrophically huge economic crash. Society is far more leveraged than it was a few years ago, and this debt is also riskier, as a sizeable portion comes from private credit. That is a paper tower, waiting to fall. Something as simple as a disruption in the job market could cause a chain-reaction of defaulting debt. ==Oh no wait, that is just what AI will do!==

Indeed, UBS has even highlighted that a widespread “AI shock” can severely hit the banking industry through many avenues, but particularly through a rising rate of consumers defaulting on private credit debt as they lose jobs to AI.

In other words, if AI does work as planned, and we do see the job market shake up, and AI replacement of labour, that this gargantuan AI investment is inherently betting will happen, there will be another credit crunch, and most likely another recession.

Great! We are screwed either way.

### Summary

There is a reason big tech has made this catastrophic bet (AI accelerationism to concentrate power), and there are ways we can remedy this situation (regulation and more progressive politics). But those are conversations for another day. The fact that it is increasingly clear this AI bubble has no positive outcome warrants a pause for thought. This is one of those occasions where I hope I am wrong, missed some key detail, or don’t have the right context. Not so that the tech bros can buy another yacht. But so that you and I don’t have to go through hell.
