## A grim history of Popes who told leaders to go pound sand.

![The Pentagon Tried To Bully The Pope. History Says That’s Cute](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*ux2I8gAxe_bnadFFJb0YDA.jpeg)

Henry at Canossa, history painting by Eduard Schwoiser \[de\] (1862) | Public Domain

In 1801, Napoleon Bonaparte gave his envoy to the Vatican a single instruction: “Treat the Pope as though he had an army of 200,000 men.”

Napoleon did not say this because the pope actually had 200,000 men. The pope had approximately zero men. What the pope had was something Napoleon, for all his megalomania, was smart enough to recognize he lacked: Moral authority. Legitimacy. The ability to make an emperor look like an emperor…or make him look like a thug.

So Napoleon did what any emperor does when faced with a leader no army can defeat. He dragged Pius VII to Paris like a prop for his coronation and placed the crown on his own head. (Apparently, the show was rehearsed ahead of time.)

But what he didn’t do was make the mistake of thinking the Church didn’t matter.

Which brings us to this week, with rumors circulating that the Pentagon and the Vatican publicly disagreed about what happened in a closed-door meeting in January. The Pentagon called it “respectful and professional.” Vatican sources have used words like “unpleasant” and “confrontational.”

Here is what is not in dispute: Pope Leo has called the president’s threat to destroy “an entire civilization” in Iran “truly unacceptable.” He has told Catholics to call their congressmen. And on Palm Sunday, he said that ==God “does not listen to the prayers of those who wage war.”==

Whatever happened in that room, the pope is not giving his tacit approval to another genocide. I wonder why

==History has some answers. Popes have been dealing with men who think military power is the only power that matters since roughly the fourth century. And the record is — let’s say — mixed for the men with the armies.==

Here are seven popes who told the most powerful men on earth to go pound sand.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1100/format:webp/1*lG3_AxmyqQthRrOxnDmZEA.jpeg)

### Gregory VII vs. Henry IV (1077): The Pope Who Made an Emperor Stand Barefoot in a Blizzard

In 1076, Holy Roman Emperor Henry IV — the most powerful man in Europe, commander of armies, anointed by God, etc. etc. — decided he’d had enough of Pope Gregory VII telling him he couldn’t hand-pick his own bishops. So he assembled his German bishops, declared Gregory deposed, and fired off a letter that ended: “I, Henry, king by the grace of God, with all of my bishops, say to you: come down, come down, and be damned throughout the ages.”

The Pope wasn’t having it. His response was to excommunicate Henry and release every one of his subjects from their oaths of loyalty. Which, in the eleventh century, was the political equivalent of pulling the pin on a grenade and handing it back.

Then, the pope gave Henry IV one year to repent or lose his throne permanently. His nobles began shopping for a replacement king and informed Henry that unless he secured absolution by early 1077, the jig was up.

Facing a deadline, Henry crossed the Alps in the dead of winter with his wife and infant son, nearly dying in the mountain passes, and arrived at Canossa Castle, where Gregory was staying as a guest. The pope made him wait outside. In the snow. Barefoot. Wearing a hair shirt. For three days.

Gregory was inside the castle. Warm.

On the fourth day, Gregory opened the gates, accepted Henry’s penance, and lifted the excommunication.

The delicate truce didn’t last. Henry went home, rebuilt his army, invaded Rome, and installed his own pope. Gregory died in exile.

But “going to Canossa” entered the language as a synonym for humiliation so enduring that eight hundred years later, Otto von Bismarck would declare before the German parliament: “We are not going to Canossa!”

**Scorecard:** Pope wins the narrative. Emperor wins the war. Everybody dies unhappy. As God intended.

### Innocent III vs. King John of England (1208–1214): The Pope Who Repossessed an Entire Country

When King John of England refused to accept the pope’s choice for Archbishop of Canterbury, Innocent III did not write a strongly worded letter. He placed the entire kingdom of England under interdict. For six years, no church bells rang. No marriages were blessed. No last rites were administered. The dead were buried in ditches without ceremony. England became, spiritually speaking, a parking lot.

John responded by seizing Church property and banishing clergy who complied with the interdict. Innocent escalated by excommunicating John and (this is where it gets fun) he declared John deposed and authorized King Philip II of France to invade England and take the throne.

John, facing a French invasion, a baronial revolt, and the existential dread of a medieval king who has been told his soul is going to hell, caved completely. He didn’t just submit. He surrendered England to the pope as a papal fief and agreed to pay an annual tribute of 1,000 marks.

The King of England became, on paper, the pope’s tenant.

Incidentally, Innocent III ran this play on multiple monarchs simultaneously. He also excommunicated the Holy Roman Emperor and put France under interdict. The man treated Europe like a chessboard and kings like pawns who hadn’t yet realized they were pawns.

**Scorecard:** Pope wins. England literally becomes Church property. John goes on to have such a bad reign that his barons force him to sign the Magna Carta, which is the only reason anyone remembers him fondly, and even that’s generous.

### Boniface VIII vs. Philip IV of France (1302): The Pope Who Claimed Dominion Over Every Human on Earth (Then Got Slapped)

Boniface VIII was not a subtle man. When Philip IV of France started taxing the clergy without papal permission, Boniface issued *Unam Sanctam* — the single most audacious document in the history of papal overreach, which declared that “it is absolutely necessary for salvation that every human creature be subject to the Roman Pontiff.”

Philip’s response was to send his lawyer, Guillaume de Nogaret, to Italy with a small armed force. They broke into the papal residence at Anagni and, according to multiple contemporary accounts, physically assaulted the sixty-eight-year-old pope. One account says Nogaret slapped him across the face. Boniface reportedly responded: “Here is my neck, here is my head. I will at least die as pope.”

He was rescued by townspeople after three days, but never recovered. He died a month later — of humiliation, his enemies said, or of old age, his allies said. Either way, the papacy was so weakened by the episode that within five years, the French crown had moved the entire papal court to Avignon, beginning the period known as the Babylonian Captivity.

**Scorecard:** Pope loses. Badly. But the theological claim of *Unam Sanctam* was never formally retracted and remains, technically, on the books. The Catholic Church plays the long game.

### Julius II vs. Everyone (1503–1513): The Pope Who Put on Armor and Did It Himself

Pope Julius II got tired of waiting for secular rulers to do what he wanted, so he did what no pope before or since has done: he put on a suit of armor, mounted a horse, and personally led troops into battle.

At the siege of Mirandola in 1511, the sixty-seven-year-old pope inspected the trenches in the snow, screamed at his engineers to dig faster, and when the walls were finally breached, reportedly climbed through the gap himself because the gate wasn’t opening fast enough.

He didn’t stop there. He commissioned the new St. Peter’s Basilica because the old one wasn’t grand enough. He bullied Michelangelo into painting the Sistine Chapel ceiling. (When Michelangelo didn’t paint fast enough, Julius climbed the scaffolding and beat Michelangelo with his stick.)

Erasmus, the great humanist, was so appalled that he wrote a satire imagining Julius arriving at the gates of heaven in full armor, and St. Peter refusing to let him in because he didn’t recognize him as a man of God.

**Scorecard:** Pope wins every fight he picks. Leaves behind the Sistine Chapel and St. Peter’s Basilica. Goes to meet his Maker dressed for war. Let the theologians sort it out.

### Leo X vs. His Own Cardinals (1517): The Medici Pope Who Turned an Assassination Plot into a Hostile Takeover

Alfonso Petrucci was furious that Leo had deposed his brother from power in Siena. So he teamed up with a group of cardinals and allegedly conspired to murder the pope by poisoning the bandages on his anal fistula (yes, really…he literally tried to poison his arse.) Leo X did not panic. He did not pray. He did not forgive.

He had Petrucci lured back to Rome under a promise of safe conduct, arrested him the moment he arrived, extracted confessions under torture, and had Petrucci strangled in his cell. (The strangling was performed by a Moor, because it was considered improper for a Christian to kill a prince of the Church. People are funny like that.)

Then — and this is the move — Leo used the conspiracy as a pretext to appoint thirty-one new cardinals in a single day, doubling the size of the College of Cardinals overnight and flooding it with Medici allies, Florentine loyalists, and men who owed him everything. It was a corporate restructuring disguised as justice. Some historians believe Leo exaggerated or even fabricated the conspiracy specifically to engineer this power grab. Well played, Pope. Well played.

Five months later, an Augustinian friar named Martin Luther nailed ninety-five theses to a church door in Wittenberg, and Leo’s problems got considerably worse. But for one glorious afternoon in July 1517, a Medici pope turned an assassination attempt into the most brazen patronage play in Church history.

**Scorecard:** Pope wins the battle, loses the Reformation. Which, in terms of unintended consequences, is like winning a hand of poker and accidentally setting the casino on fire.

### Pius VII vs. Napoleon (1809): The Pope Who Got Kidnapped and Still Won

Napoleon wanted what every strongman wants from the Church: legitimacy on his terms.

But when Napoleon annexed the Papal States in 1809 and demanded Pius’s political submission, the pope excommunicated him. Napoleon’s response was to have Pius arrested in the middle of the night and dragged across Italy and into France, where he was held prisoner for five years.

Pius refused to cooperate. He refused to appoint the bishops Napoleon wanted. He refused to validate Napoleon’s second marriage. He refused to do anything, really, except sit there and be the pope, which turns out to be a surprisingly effective strategy when your captor needs you more than you need him. Napoleon reportedly complained that Pius was “a lamb, a truly good man, whom I respect and revere,” but who was “as immovable as a mule” on matters of principle.

Napoleon lost his empire. Pius returned to Rome in triumph. The Papal States were restored at the Congress of Vienna. The man with zero divisions outlasted the man who conquered Europe.

**Scorecard:** Pope wins. Slowly, quietly, stubbornly. The lamb eats the lion.

### John Paul II vs. the Soviet Union (1978–1989): The Pope Who Answered Stalin’s Question

When Stalin famously sneered, “The Pope‚ how many divisions has he got” he meant it as a dismissal. Forty years later, a Polish pope provided the answer: enough.

John Paul II didn’t command armies. He commanded something the Soviets couldn’t build a wall against: the loyalty of forty million Polish Catholics who suddenly had one of their own on the throne of St. Peter. His 1979 visit to Poland drew millions into the streets. The Solidarity movement — the first independent labor union in a Soviet bloc country — was born the following year, with the Church providing its organizational backbone, its moral language, and its sanctuary.

When the Soviets considered invading Poland to crush Solidarity, John Paul reportedly sent word to the Kremlin that if Soviet tanks rolled into Warsaw, he would fly to Poland and stand in front of them personally. The Soviets blinked.

A decade later, the Berlin Wall fell. The Soviet Union dissolved. John Paul II, who had survived an assassination attempt widely believed to have been connected to Soviet intelligence, stood in the ruins of the empire that had mocked the papacy’s irrelevance.

**Scorecard:** Pope wins. Soviet Union ceases to exist. Stalin’s divisions are, at last count, zero.

The record, as promised, is mixed. Popes have humiliated emperors and been slapped by lawyers. They’ve outlasted superpowers and been dragged into captivity. They’ve played the long game brilliantly and occasionally set their own house on fire in the process.

==But here’s the pattern that holds across a thousand years: every leader who tried to bend the papacy to his will either failed outright or succeeded temporarily at catastrophic cost.== Philip IV got his Avignon popes — and got a Reformation. Napoleon got his coronation — and died on an island. Stalin got his punchline — and got a Polish pope who helped bury his empire. The Church absorbs the blow, survives the humiliation, and waits.

The Catholic Church is very, very good at waiting.

The moral lesson, if you want one, cuts both ways. For the men with the armies: the pope doesn’t need divisions. He needs a balcony, a microphone, and the willingness to say what you did was wrong — and that’s a weapon no defense budget can neutralize.

For the men in the miters: moral authority is not a permanent possession. It can be spent, squandered, or corrupted from within. Ask Boniface. Ask Leo X. Ask every Avignon pope who traded the gospel for a French king’s protection. The Church’s power has always depended on its willingness to be the thing that tells the emperor he has no clothes — and the moment it starts selling clothes to the emperor instead, the whole thing collapses.

That diplomatic collapse is happening now. Rumor has it that during the recent meeting between the Vatican and the White House, Undersecretary of Defense Elbridge Colby referenced the displaced Avignon popes — a thinly veiled threat that Pope Leo should play sock puppet to Trump.

Pope Leo XIV appears to understand the assignment. ==He’s not excommunicating anyone.== (Although no one would flinch if he did.) He’s not putting on armor. He’s doing something much harder to counter: he’s being publicly, quietly, immovably right — and letting the most powerful government on earth decide how it wants to look standing next to him.

Napoleon would have understood the problem immediately. ==One suspects the Pentagon is still working on it.==
