## 1. Start with a system, not personal taste

Use the color wheel as the foundation for palette decisions. Pick a color relationship first, then adapt it to the project.

## 2. Choose one harmony model on purpose

### Monochromatic

- Best for clean, safe, brand-focused palettes
- Build variation with tints, shades, and tones of one hue
- Add contrast carefully so the design does not become flat or boring

### Analogous

- Best for calm, cohesive, low-friction visuals
- Use neighboring hues on the wheel
- Assign one dominant color, one supporting color, and one accent
- Avoid choosing hues that are so close together they become muddy or dull

### Complementary

- Best for strong contrast and high-visibility emphasis
- Use opposite hues on the wheel
- Great for calls to action, highlights, clickable elements, and focal points
- Use with restraint because it can become loud or jarring

### Split-complementary

- Best when you want contrast without the harshness of a direct complementary pair
- Safer than pure complementary for broader interfaces and layouts

### Triadic

- Best for colorful but balanced systems
- Use three evenly spaced hues
- Keep one color dominant and the others as accents
- Reduce saturation if the palette feels too competitive or noisy

### Tetradic / Double-complementary

- Best for advanced use cases only
- Powerful, but the hardest to balance
- Use only when you can control hierarchy very carefully

## 3. Build clear visual hierarchy

Do not let all colors fight for attention.

- Pick one dominant color
- Add one secondary/supporting color
- Reserve one accent color for emphasis
- A practical ratio is often:
    - 60 / 30 / 10 for primary / secondary / accent
    - or 80 / 20 when using a simpler two-color system

## 4. Control contrast with value and saturation

Color choice is not only about hue.

- Use lighter and darker values to create depth and separation
- Use saturation to control energy and softness
- Lower saturation for more refined, calm, or premium-looking interfaces
- Use highly saturated colors sparingly for emphasis

## 5. Use neutrals strategically

Black, white, and grays are not filler colors.

- Use them to restore contrast in monochrome or analogous palettes
- Use them to support readability and spacing
- Let neutrals carry structure so accent colors can do specific jobs

## 6. Match color behavior to function

Use color where it helps users understand what matters.

- Backgrounds should usually support, not dominate
- Accent colors should mark actions, alerts, or key information
- Strong contrast is useful for important interactive elements
- Do not spread your brightest color everywhere

## 7. Design for emotion, brand, and audience

Pick colors that fit the message and positioning.

- Choose hues that support the brand’s intended emotion and personality
- Consider whether you want to feel calm, energetic, trustworthy, playful, premium, or urgent
- Study the category first so you know when to align with conventions and when to stand apart

## 8. Respect context and culture

Color meaning is not universal.

- Check how your palette may be read in different cultures or markets
- Verify that symbolic meanings do not conflict with the message
- Avoid assuming one region’s interpretation applies everywhere

## 9. Test colors in the real medium

A palette can look right in one medium and wrong in another.

- Use RGB thinking for screen-based products
- Use CMYK thinking for print
- Expect colors to shift between digital screens, print, materials, textures, and lighting conditions
- Test before finalizing

## 10. Keep the palette disciplined

More colors do not automatically improve a design.

- Limit the number of active colors
- Use odd-number groupings when you want a more dynamic feel
- Avoid adding extra hues unless they serve a role
- Consistency matters more than novelty

## 11. Use practical safeguards

Before shipping a palette, ask:

- Is there one obvious dominant color?
- Are accents rare enough to feel important?
- Is there enough contrast to guide attention?
- Does the palette still work when desaturated, darkened, or printed?
- Does the mood match the product and audience?

## 12. Best default workflow

1. Define the brand mood and user task
2. Choose one harmony model
3. Pick a dominant hue
4. Add a supporting hue
5. Add one restrained accent
6. Tune value and saturation
7. Add neutrals for readability and structure
8. Test on the real medium and audience context
9. Simplify until every color has a job
