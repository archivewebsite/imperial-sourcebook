## Study Material Summary Notes

**Journal:** _Do Attention and Memory Tasks Require the Same Lighting? A Study in University Classrooms_

### Background

Lighting is an important factor in learning environments because it can affect students’ comfort, visibility, mood, attention, memory, and overall academic performance. Modern university classrooms involve many types of learning activities, including lectures, digital tasks, collaboration, and independent study. Because of this, the same lighting condition may not be suitable for every classroom activity.

Previous studies on lighting and cognitive performance have produced mixed results. Some research suggests that brighter light improves attention and alertness, while other studies found little or no effect. Similarly, research on memory performance under different lighting conditions has also been inconclusive. This study was designed to clarify how two lighting factors, **illuminance** and **correlated colour temperature**, affect attention and memory in university students.

### Aim of the Study

The main aim of the study was to examine whether attention and memory tasks require the same classroom lighting conditions. The researchers focused on two lighting variables:

**Illuminance** refers to the amount of light falling on a surface and is measured in lux.

**Correlated Colour Temperature**, or CCT, refers to the colour appearance of light and is measured in Kelvin. Lower CCT values create warmer, yellowish light, while higher CCT values create cooler, bluish-white light.

The study investigated how different combinations of illuminance and CCT affected students’ performance in attention and memory tasks.

### Methodology

The study used a laboratory-based experimental method. Instead of using real classrooms, the researchers created a virtual classroom using virtual reality. This allowed them to control the lighting conditions carefully and avoid interference from natural light or changes in classroom layout.

A total of **90 university students** participated in the study. The participants were mostly within the typical age range of university students and had normal or corrected-to-normal vision.

The researchers tested three levels of illuminance:

**100 lx, 300 lx, and 500 lx**

They also tested three CCT levels:

**3000 K, 4000 K, and 6500 K**

These combinations created nine different lighting scenarios. Each participant experienced the different classroom lighting conditions through a head-mounted virtual reality display.

### Virtual Reality Environment

The classroom used in the experiment was based on a real university classroom. The researchers created a simulated classroom without natural lighting so that only artificial lighting variables could be studied.

The virtual reality setup allowed the researchers to present different lighting environments in a controlled way. Participants completed cognitive tasks while experiencing each lighting condition. The researchers also measured the participants’ sense of presence in the virtual environment to confirm that the simulation felt realistic enough for the experiment.

The results showed that the participants experienced an acceptable level of presence in the virtual classroom, meaning the VR environment was suitable for testing cognitive responses.

### Cognitive Tasks

The study measured two main cognitive functions: **memory** and **attention**.

For the memory task, participants listened to audio clips containing sets of related words. After listening, they had to recall as many words as possible. Their memory performance was measured by the number of correct matches.

For the attention task, participants had to respond quickly to specific auditory stimuli by clicking a mouse. They also had to avoid responding to distractor sounds. Attention performance was measured using reaction time and number of errors.

### Results: Illuminance and Memory

The study found that memory performance was best under the lowest illuminance level, **100 lx**. As illuminance increased to 300 lx and 500 lx, memory performance decreased.

This suggests that lower lighting levels may be more suitable for memory-based tasks, such as recalling information or reviewing learned material.

### Results: Illuminance and Attention

The results for attention were different from the results for memory. The lowest illuminance level, **100 lx**, produced the worst attention performance. Under this condition, participants had slower reaction times and made more errors.

Attention performance improved under **300 lx and 500 lx**. However, there was no major difference between 300 lx and 500 lx.

This suggests that attention-based tasks require brighter lighting than memory tasks.

### Results: CCT and Memory

Memory performance improved as CCT increased. The best memory results were found under the **6500 K** lighting condition.

This means that cooler, bluish-white light supported better memory performance than warmer light at 3000 K or neutral light at 4000 K.

### Results: CCT and Attention

Attention performance also improved under higher CCT conditions. The best attention results were found at **6500 K**, where participants had faster reaction times and fewer errors.

This shows that higher CCT lighting was beneficial for both attention and memory tasks.

### Discussion

The findings show that attention and memory do not require the same illuminance level. Attention improved with higher levels of illuminance, while memory improved with lower levels of illuminance. This means that classroom lighting should be adapted depending on the type of task students are performing.

The study also found that higher CCT, especially **6500 K**, improved performance in both attention and memory tasks. This supports previous research suggesting that cooler light can improve alertness, concentration, and cognitive performance.

The results are important for classroom design because they suggest that flexible or dynamic lighting systems could improve learning conditions. Instead of using one fixed lighting setting throughout the day, classrooms could use different lighting levels depending on the activity.

For example, brighter lighting may be more useful for activities that require attention, quick responses, or active participation. Lower illuminance may be more suitable for memory-related tasks. Cooler light may generally support both attention and memory.

### Conclusion

The study concludes that classroom lighting has a significant effect on students’ cognitive performance. Attention and memory tasks require different illuminance levels. Attention improves under brighter lighting, while memory performs better under lower lighting. However, both attention and memory benefit from higher CCT, especially 6500 K.

The study supports the idea that classroom lighting should be adjustable rather than fixed. Dynamic lighting systems could help improve student performance by matching lighting conditions to different learning tasks.

### Limitations

The study had some limitations. First, the time of day was not fully controlled, even though the experiment was conducted during normal university hours. Since attention and memory can vary depending on the time of day, this may have influenced the results.

Second, the study only focused on illuminance and CCT. Other lighting factors, such as glare, uniformity, daylight, and classroom layout, were not deeply examined. Future studies could investigate how these additional factors interact with cognitive performance.
