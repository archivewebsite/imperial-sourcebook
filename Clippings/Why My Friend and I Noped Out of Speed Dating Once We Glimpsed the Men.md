## On consent, transparency, and the commodification of women

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*sHz2nuNzqvWU8VtjBVFxuQ.jpeg)

Photo by Shane Ryan Herilalaina on Unsplash

Last fall, my friend Ameliya and I were lamenting about how we wish our city offered more places to connect with single men without having to go to rowdy clubs, adopt hobbies we aren’t organically into, or sacrifice hours to apps. “One of my friends recently met a cool guy at a speed dating event. That might be a better way to find people,” I mused.

Ameliya agreed. So when she asked me a few weeks later if I’d like to attend one together, I was game.

From the age brackets on the signup website, Ameliya chose an event for singles aged 35–49. This put us in the lower half of the range while still ensuring (at least in theory) that all the men would be within about a decade of us. However, while the signup interface asked for our D.O.B., it didn’t actuallyverify our ages — nor, in hindsight, guarantee that any age verification would happen at all. It didn’t cross our minds that men might lie about their age to con their way into meeting younger women.…

which was very naïve of us.

==Because if you’re a single woman anywhere on Earth, you’ll have already seen LOTS of men on dating apps claiming to be many years younger than they are.==

Thus, you can imagine what happened when we showed up: Ameliya observed that the men who were parading in and milling about the bar area — with about one or two exceptions — looked substantially older than promised. In fact, with how old they looked compared to the *ladies* filing in, I didn’t even realize at first that these were the same ostensible 35–49-year-olds we were supposed to be meeting that night. By Amelia’s estimate, the guys were all firmly north of 50.

(And in the wry, unscientific estimation of the friend with whom we later debriefed: “Trust me, I’ve dated lots of old men — ==if they looked 50, they were at== ==*least*== ==60.”)==

The deception did not amuse us.

I could write entire essays about ==how predatory it is to lie about your age in dating,== or the patriarchy inherent when men fixate on finding a younger woman. Those are huge topics for another time. Instead, allow me to unpack the gender politics that suddenly derailed our plans, now that Ameliya and I realized we were about to spend our night on dudes who’d seemingly lied their way in the door.

She asked me a simple question that touched off a minefield of deeper, *unspoken* questions about boundaries, consent, and self-respect — themes I never even imagined might be hiding inside of something as low-stakes as speed dating:

“I’m not attracted to any of these guys,” she said. “Are you?”

Fuck.

No.

### Nowhere to Hide

I should’ve anticipated the vibe would be off when a same-day email from the organizers, despite its brevity, somehow packed in no less than *four* references to opportunities to drink at the event. My mom and I had laughed together over the phone that afternoon: “Wow, they’re really concerned about people needing drinks!”

Now it made sense:

Alcohol loosens inhibitions, a.k.a. boundaries.

Being asked point-blank, though, how I felt about our options? I instantly felt uneasy at having to spend the next few hours playing captive audience to these men’s attentions.

==See, in real life, when women (or girls) aren’t interested in a man, we often feel compelled to humor him and leave at least a== ==*faint*== ==impression of openness to his interest.== This isn’t because we enjoy giving our time or energy to guys we don’t want, much less that women are evil liars who “just wantattention” (as the manosphere accuses).

No. When women fawn, it’s because we’re *afraid* to reject men — and the more entitled a man behaves in peacocking or pressing for our company, the more likely he is to be dangerous.

This is why, in naturalsettings, I try to disengage as promptly and non-confrontationally as possible. For instance, I might: 1) sidestep a guy’s approach altogether, 2) lie and say I have a boyfriend (because dangerous men respect other men more than they respect women — even if those other men are imaginary), or 3) just exchange numbers to end the interaction quicker and then reject him from a safe distance via text.

Alternatively, after the perfunctory exchange of names, I say, “Sorry, I have to get to an appointment,” or just abscond to go interact with someone else. (My proudest example: once, at a mixer, I escaped a guy’s attentions by stepping away after the first minute or so to go commiserate with a friend’s dad about the joys of constipation. Copy my technique, ladies!)

==This is how, in daily life, I can usually avoid wasting my time on stroking the egos of men I don’t want.==

At speed dating, though… these options wouldn’t exist: I couldn’t claim to be busy or unavailable; everyone was there *because* they were free and available. I also couldn’t just wander off to interact with someone else; the schedule was designed to ensure each person an equal chunk of our time.

There was no escape hatch.

### The Fatigue of Fawning

Since the entire premise and implicit promise of speed dating isthat you’ll offer a contingent of strangers your undivided attention — regardless of how viscerally you do not want them — there’s a certain social pressure to chat your way politely through the rotation, reserving all rejections (or approval) for afterwards, via the organizers. ==I wanted to spare these men the humiliation of realizing they were unwanted…== yet hiding this fact from them would require that I pretend to be sufficiently enthused to chat with them in the first place.

It’s hard enough to feign and fawn for someone you don’t want for six minutes (the allotted time per “date”).

It’s immeasurably harder to keep feigning and fawning, with man after man, for *hours* — especially since much of women’s trauma is precisely *about* feigning and fawning for men we don’t want.

The more I envisioned this night unfolding, the more my anxiety crawled. I’m too sincere a person (and have overcome too much trauma) to be capable of bluffing that long. At best, I’d waste the night on banal conversation. At worst, I might get so fatigued by the effort that I’d become unable to maintain the charade.

… and I hated the possibility that my failure to mask might hurt anyone’s feelings.

### The Indignity of Deception and the Unpaid Girlfriend Experience

The more I tried to puzzle out a strategy that might help me fake the lighthearted vibe that the occasion demanded — and appease these men I had no interest in — the more I felt not just stressed but disgusted.

And betrayed:

==Our money had been collected in exchange for an apparently false premise (i.e., age-matched partners), while== ==*theirs*== ==was collected for… access to us.==

As Ameliya and I hung back from the crowd pre-event, hashing out our reservations in hushed tones, I considered staying, just to “get our money’s worth.” Yet it dawned on me that, if we stayed, the opposite would happen:

When a man seeks a woman’s companionship — without being transparent about details that might nullify her consent — then the encounter isn’t consensual. True consent is *informed*, and being informed requires truthful information. Hence why it’s unethical to angle for access to a date while misrepresenting yourself.

And why, if you want an evening of pleasant chatter and personal validation from younger women who aren’t interested, the ethical thing for you to do is to *pay* someone for the favor: a sex worker.

When a woman must don the role of Gracious Companion strictly to serve your one-sided needs, this is an act of emotional labor — and she absolutely deserves to be paid for that. *Not recruited into a dynamic that violates her preferences* ***and*** *charges her a fee.*

The prospect of moving down this assembly line of “dates” with guys who didn’t even seem to want to tell the truth about basic biometric data made me feel gross, like we ladies were being trotted out by some third party who recognized us as lucrative yet didn’t care enough about our boundaries to enforce them. Kind of like when dance clubs offer discounts on “ladies’ nights” to draw us in (management knows that a club packed with women is a magnet for men’s money), and yet where we end up treated as masturbatory aids out on the floor by guys who rub their erections against us, trying to pass sexual assault off as “dance.”

### The Gendered Socialization to Ignore Our Desires

Finally, the feminist in me cringes to admit this, but despite all our justified anxiety, annoyance, and dismay, Ameliya and I also realized we felt… guilt.

For having the “audacity” to be uninterested — to have preferences — in the first place.

Did it make us shallow that we didn’t *want* to meet the men we saw?

Would it be wrong to reject them before “giving them a chance”?

Was it a moral failing to be so unattracted to any given guy — hell, a whole cadre of them — that we just didn’t want to play along?

Consider the rom-coms of the ’00s, when Ameliya and I were coming of age: rom-coms often paired modelesque women with non-modelesque men and illustrated female “character growth” through stories wherein a woman must learn to *give up on* a man she loved or found attractive, in favor of acquiescing to some guy who never really struck her fancy at all.

This latter character was always painted as the “correct” party in the dynamic: he wasn’tsomeone *she* wanted, but *he* wanted *her* — and since society prioritizes men’s preferences while treating women’s preferences as silly and up for debate (even our preferences over something as essential as our very *internal organs*)… the unwanted man always *deserved* what he desired — and the woman was only “complete” once she realized that he was entitled to receive it.

“It” being her.

In real life as well, men almost always find cultural approval for desiring the women they find appealing, yet women are often discouraged from, if not outright condemned for, having physical preferences of our own (lest we be called “shallow”), while we are *simultaneously* expected to decorate ourselves so that our acquisition can serve men’s status and validation.

==From girlhood, therefore, we are urged to “just give him a chance” whenever a man is assertive enough to make a move,== and that, if we aren’t attracted to or interested in him, we should often still entertain his attentions with grace and gratitude anyway.

Just in case, in time, he can successfully win us over.

*As if we owe men* the opportunity to win us over.

To consume us.

Perhaps you’re thinking:

==*Wow, how absurd to feel so triggered by the prospect of a night of chit-chat with strangers!*==

But this is what patriarchal trauma does to women; it robs us of the chance to feel at ease about casual friendliness towards men, and when we do “loosen up,” we often receive negative reinforcement in the form of danger, harm, and/or blame. It’s frightening to risk that in connecting with *any* man, which is why we’re even less inclined to risk it on men we don’t *want* to connect with.

Likewise, you might protest:

==*But it’s not like you were forced to DO anything with these men!*==

That’s true. Yet we were still deceived, apparently, by men who felt so entitled to the attentions of younger ladies that they had no qualms about lying to meet us: we’d shown up on the promise that we’d be introduced to men of a certain description and were instead expected to interact with ones who didn’t deem even our *simplest* preference — age — worthy of respecting.

If you can’t see how that’s pathological and predatory, I can’t help you.

Ameliya and I waited around the venue for nearly an *hour* as we wrestled with what to do, and what our decision would “mean” about us, until we finally decided to…

Honor our boundaries by leaving.

Decline to participate at all.

Walking out felt like a manifesto of self-worth.

We spent the rest of our night at a chill bar across town, debriefing about *why* exactly this speed dating event had felt so unexpectedly gross, reassuring each other that we are entitled to the dignity of our own boundaries, and, despite our failed mission, reaffirming our hope to find love someday with men who respect us.

… and don’t lie about their age.
