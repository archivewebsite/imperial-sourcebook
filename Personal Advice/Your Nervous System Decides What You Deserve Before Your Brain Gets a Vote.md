## I was doing everything right. Thinking positive, working hard, showing up every day. But my body was running a completely different program.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/0*H_pNUx8sxgKAPy3C)

Photo by Jozsef Hocza on Unsplash

There’s a version of this story that sounds inspirational.

Guy works hard. Guy stays positive. Guy believes in himself, does the affirmations, reads the books, meditates every morning, journals about gratitude, visualizes the life he wants with enough specificity to furnish every room.

Guy doesn’t get the thing.

The inspirational version says he wasn’t aligned, or the timing was off, or the universe had something better planned. The inspirational version wraps failure in a ribbon and calls it redirection.

I want to tell you the version that isn’t inspirational.

The version where I was doing everything right and the thing that stopped me was my own body, running a program I didn’t know was installed, sabotaging every open door while my conscious mind was busy holding the welcome sign.

### 1/ The year of contradiction

I went through a stretch, about two years ago, where I was the most disciplined I’d ever been.

Woke up at 5 AM. Wrote every morning. Meditated for twenty minutes.

Read books on manifestation and mindset and the kind of spiritual self-help that makes you feel like you’ve cracked the code to getting what you want. I had a vision board on my wall.

I had a journal where I wrote what I was grateful for. I had a list of goals written in present tense, as though they’d already happened, because that’s what the books told me to do.

*I am a successful storyteller.* *I am financially free.* *I am living my creative purpose.*

I wrote those sentences every morning. I believed them. Or I thought I believed them.

And then something would happen.

A client would show interest in a big project, exactly the kind of thing I’d been visualizing, and I’d feel my chest tighten. My emails would get slower. My responses would get more cautious.

I’d introduce caveats and qualifications and subtle little exit ramps into the conversation that I didn’t realize I was building until the opportunity had driven right past me and out the other side.

A friend would introduce me to someone who could change my career trajectory, and instead of leaning in, I’d feel something in my stomach pull back.

The conversation would go well on the surface, but I’d leave my number and then feel a wave of nausea when my phone rang two days later.

I was chasing the thing with my mind and running from it with my body.

And I couldn’t understand why, because I was doing *everything right.* The morning routine. The affirmations. The gratitude journal. The meditation. The work. I was ticking every box the self-help industry had given me, and I was still, somehow, standing in my own way.

> *“The cruelest form of self-sabotage is the kind that looks like preparation. You do all the work and then watch your body refuse to collect the paycheck.”*

### 2/ The language your body speaks

A therapist I was seeing at the time said something that rearranged my understanding of everything.

She said: ==*“Your mind believes you deserve this. Your nervous system doesn’t. And your nervous system is older, faster, and louder than your mind. It will always win.”*==

She explained it simply. When you grow up in an environment where good things are unreliable, where stability can disappear without warning, where approval comes with conditions and love comes with asterisks, your nervous system learns a lesson.

It learns that receiving is dangerous, that the safest position is slightly withdrawn, slightly guarded, always ready to pull back.

And this lesson lives in your body.

The tightness in your chest when something good is about to happen. The impulse to check your phone during a conversation that could change your life.

The way you sabotage a deadline by getting sick the week before, or start a fight with your partner the night before a big meeting, or find seventeen reasons to doubt a project that your mind knows is right.

Your thoughts can be reprogrammed in weeks. Write the affirmation. Say it with feeling. Visualize the outcome. Your conscious mind is remarkably cooperative.

Your nervous system takes years. Because it doesn’t speak in words.

It speaks in heat and cold, in tension and release, in the way your stomach drops when someone offers you the thing you’ve been asking for and some part of you screams *put it down before it disappears.*

I’d been trying to reprogram my life using a language my body didn’t understand.

I was writing love letters to my future while my nervous system was still stuck in a past where receiving meant vulnerability and vulnerability meant pain.

> *“You can think positive thoughts every morning for a year. But if your body doesn’t feel safe to receive what you’re asking for, your body will find a way to give it back.”*

### 3/ Learning to feel safe

The shift came from somewhere I didn’t expect.

A conversation I had with myself at 6 AM on a morning when the writing wasn’t coming and I was staring at my journal, pen in hand, feeling the familiar tightness in my chest that I’d learned to recognize as the doubt reflex.

Instead of pushing through it, I did something different. I put the pen down. I closed my eyes. And I asked the tightness a question.

*What are you afraid of?*

That sounds woo-woo. I know. But what came up wasn’t woo-woo at all.

A specific memory surfaced. I was twelve, standing in a room, having just done well in something. I don’t even remember what. A competition, a test, something I’d won. And instead of feeling joy, I felt fear. Because in my household, good things were temporary.

Approval had conditions.

The high of achievement was always, always followed by the weight of expectation. Do it again. Do it better. Don’t get comfortable.

My body learned, at twelve, that receiving is the beginning of a demand. That the moment you accept something good, the clock starts on the next thing you owe.

And twenty years later, that twelve-year-old was still running the show.

Every time an opportunity showed up, every time a door opened, he’d feel the tightness and reach for the exit. Not because the opportunity was bad, but because receiving it felt like signing a contract his body had learned to distrust.

The work, the real work, had nothing to do with manifestation or mindset or thinking positive.

The work was sitting with that twelve-year-old and letting him know, slowly, imperfectly, morning by morning, that it was safe to have open hands. That good things could arrive without a bill attached. That he could hold something and keep it.

That work is slow. It won’t fit on a vision board and nobody will post about it on Instagram.

But it’s the only work that actually changes what you’re able to receive.

> *“Manifestation without nervous system safety is like building a house and locking the front door from the inside. The guests are standing outside. You just won’t let them in.”*

### Last

I still do the morning routine. I still meditate. I still write in the journal, still set intentions, still do the things that the self-help books recommend.

But I’ve added something no one talks about. Before the affirmation, before the visualization, before the gratitude list, I check in with my body. I put my hand on my chest and I ask the only question that actually matters.

*Do you feel safe right now?*

Some mornings, the answer is yes. And on those mornings, the writing flows, the ideas come, the emails get sent, and the doors that open stay open.

Some mornings, the answer is no. And on those mornings, I don’t push. I don’t force the affirmation through clenched teeth. I sit with the no. I let it be there. I let the twelve-year-old have his moment without telling him he’s wrong.

Because the biggest lie the self-help world ever sold me was that belief is a mental event. It isn’t. Belief is a full-body experience. Your mind can say *I deserve this* a thousand times, and if your body flinches when it arrives, you’ll find a way to send it back.

The universe doesn’t work in contradictions. If you think it but don’t feel it, the feeling wins. Every time.

The vision board was never the real work. Teaching my body that it was safe to want something and actually have it — that was.
